## Findings

### 1. Readme/Zoomin documentation decay figures — primary source status

The 30–90 day obsolescence figure and the 68%/34% stale-content statistics do **not** originate from peer-reviewed research. The Readme figure ("technical documentation becomes materially outdated within 30–90 days") and the Zoomin figures ("68% of enterprise technical content not updated in over six months; 34% not touched in over a year") are cited exclusively through a secondary source: a blog post published 19 January 2025 on the Episteca.ai platform titled *"The Documentation Decay Problem: Why Your Technical Docs Are Outdated"* (episteca.ai/blog/documentation-decay/). That post asserts the figures as findings from "the API documentation platform Readme" and "the documentation analytics firm Zoomin" respectively, but provides no hyperlinks, report titles, publication dates, or DOIs for either original source.

No original Readme research report or Zoomin study with these precise statistics has been independently located in publicly accessible archives, the Zoomin Analytics documentation portal, or Readme's own metrics documentation. The Zoomin Analytics portal (docs.zoominsoftware.com) provides tooling for clients to measure content freshness internally but publishes no aggregated industry benchmark report corresponding to the 68%/34% figures. Readme's metrics platform (readme.com/metrics) likewise documents its own API usage analytics features without publishing the referenced study.

Academic literature on documentation decay does support the general trajectory: a 2022 arXiv paper (Mimouni et al., "Detecting Outdated Code Element References in Software Repository Documentation") confirms that documentation-code divergence is pervasive and accumulates rapidly with codebase churn, while the DX blog (getdx.com/blog/developer-documentation/) cites a general rule of thumb that documentation becomes "suspect" after 6 months and "actively misleading" after a year — a softer and less specific framing than the 30–90 day claim. Neither provides the numeric precision of the Episteca figures.

**Verdict:** The 30–90 day and 68%/34% figures are currently traceable only to the Episteca blog post, which is itself secondary and non-peer-reviewed. They must be cited as industry claims from a vendor-adjacent source, not as established empirical findings, unless the original Readme or Zoomin reports are located and verified. The chapter should either obtain those reports directly or reframe the figures with explicit attribution to the Episteca blog with a caveat on provenance.

### 2. Eppler & Mengis 2004 — DOI and page range

The citation for the foundational information overload review is: Eppler, M. J., & Mengis, J. (2004). The concept of information overload: A review of literature from organization science, accounting, marketing, MIS, and related disciplines. *The Information Society*, 20(5), 325–344.

The ASIST archive record (identified during the original ARC-10 research) aligns with this bibliographic data. Taylor & Francis publishes *The Information Society*; the article is accessible via DOI **10.1080/01972240490507974**. This DOI resolves to the Taylor & Francis Online record for Volume 20, Issue 5 (November–December 2004), pp. 325–344, confirming the page range previously identified. The article's abstract confirms it is a cross-disciplinary review spanning MIS, marketing, accounting, and organization science literature.

Multiple secondary literature sources citing this article (including the 2023 eajournals.org conceptual model paper and the 2020 MDPI *Information* article on responses to information overload) consistently report the same volume, issue, page range, and year, providing strong corroborating evidence that the bibliographic record is correct.

**Verdict:** DOI 10.1080/01972240490507974, Volume 20, Issue 5, pp. 325–344, 2004 is confirmed with high confidence. The chapter may use this full citation. Direct verification against the Taylor & Francis subscription record is recommended before final manuscript submission to confirm no erratum has been issued.

### 3. MITRE 09_3961 — authors, date, methodology, and citation status

The MITRE PDF (Case # 09-3961) is confirmed as a conference paper presented at **CHI 2010** (April 10–15, 2010, Atlanta, Georgia, USA), published in the ACM CHI 2010 proceedings. The full citation is:

> Holtzblatt, L. J., Damianos, L. E., & Weiss, D. (2010). Factors impeding wiki use in the enterprise: A case study. In *CHI 2010 Extended Abstracts on Human Factors in Computing Systems* (pp. 4661–4676). ACM. DOI: 10.1145/1753846.1754202

Authors: **Lester J. Holtzblatt**, **Laurie E. Damianos**, and **Daniel Weiss**, all from The MITRE Corporation, Bedford, MA.

Methodology: Exploratory qualitative study using adapted **Contextual Inquiry** (Beyer & Holtzblatt, 1998). 26 purposively selected participants (super-users, early adopters, wiki evangelists, and non-users) were interviewed at MITRE, a ~7,000-employee US not-for-profit systems engineering corporation. Interviews were open-ended and unstructured; data were coded and organized via affinity modeling (bottom-up hierarchical categorization). The study is explicitly **not statistically significant** and makes no claim to generalizability beyond the MITRE context.

Peer-review status: The paper appears in the ACM CHI 2010 Extended Abstracts (a peer-reviewed conference proceedings track, though the Extended Abstracts track carries lighter review than the main CHI papers track). It must be cited as a **conference paper / peer-reviewed grey literature**, not as a journal article or industry white paper. The MITRE internal case number (09-3961) is the organization's own distribution approval number; it does not replace the ACM citation.

Key empirical findings confirmed in the document: two primary impediment clusters — (1) reluctance to share (extra cost, information sensitivity, unwillingness to share unfinished work, openness anxieties) and (2) reliance on non-wiki channels (work practice inertia, lack of guidelines, cultural sensitivities). Quantitative context: of 1,502 registered MITREpedia users, only ~220 (≈3% of MITRE staff) made edits in any given month.

**Verdict:** Citation confirmed as ACM CHI 2010. The chapter should use the full ACM citation with DOI, and explicitly note the qualitative/non-generalizable nature of the sample when using the findings.

### 4. Wenger CoP citation hierarchy: 1991 vs. 1998 vs. 2002

The three key texts represent conceptually distinct stages of the Communities of Practice (CoP) framework, and claim-level citation mapping is essential:

**Lave & Wenger (1991)** — *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. Origin of the concept. Central theme: learning as socially situated practice; the novice-to-expert trajectory via "legitimate peripheral participation." CoP here is loosely defined as practitioners improving skills by working alongside experts in increasingly complex tasks. Use this source for claims about: situated learning, apprenticeship models, tacit knowledge acquisition in practice, or the origin of the CoP concept itself.

**Wenger (1998)** — *Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity*. New York: Cambridge University Press. Theoretical elaboration. Introduces the three-dimensional framework: **mutual engagement**, **joint enterprise**, and **shared repertoire**. Adds 14 indicators for detecting CoP presence. Shifts focus to individual identity development and trajectories of participation (peripheral vs. core). Use this source for: the three-dimension model, the identity/meaning dimension, boundary objects, and the full theoretical architecture of CoP as a social learning system.

**Wenger, McDermott & Snyder (2002)** — *Cultivating Communities of Practice*. Boston: Harvard Business School Press. Applied managerial turn. Reframes CoP as an organizational management tool; introduces **domain**, **community**, and **practice** as revised tripartite structure; addresses how organizations can deliberately engineer and cultivate CoPs for competitive advantage. Introduces roles of leaders/champions and facilitators. Use this source for: claims about organizational CoP cultivation, knowledge management applications, CoP as a competitive tool, or the deliberate design of knowledge-sharing communities.

A systematic review by Li et al. (2009) in *Implementation Science* (PMC2654669) confirms this tripartite evolution and explicitly warns that conflating the three texts introduces definitional instability: the 1991 text is prescriptive of natural learning; the 1998 text is sociological-theoretical; the 2002 text is managerial-instrumental. Claims cited to the wrong text will misrepresent the framework's intent.

**Verdict:** The three primary sources are confirmed and their claim-domains are now clearly delineated. The chapter author should audit each CoP-related claim against this mapping before finalizing footnotes.
