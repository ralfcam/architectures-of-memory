## Item 1 — Jordan 1986 ICS Report: canonical title and publication status

**Verdict: CONFIRMED. Canonical title is *Serial Order: A Parallel Distributed Processing Approach*. Report number is ICS 8604, May 1986. Never published in a peer-reviewed journal in its original form.**

Four independent PDF copies of the original report confirm the cover page details:

- cseweb.ucsd.edu/~gary/PAPER-SUGGESTIONS/Jordan-TR-8604-OCRed.pdf (UCSD Computer Science)
- papers.baulab.info/papers/also/Jordan-1986.pdf
- iiif.library.cmu.edu (Carnegie Mellon Simon Archive)
- ERIC database record ED276754 (eric.ed.gov)

All four confirm:
> **Title:** Serial Order: A Parallel Distributed Processing Approach  
> **Author:** Michael I. Jordan  
> **Date:** May 1986  
> **Report number:** ICS Report 8604  
> **Institution:** Institute for Cognitive Science, University of California, San Diego, La Jolla, CA 92093  
> **Funding:** Approved for public release; distribution unlimited (US Government / DARPA-funded)

The alternate title *Attractor Dynamics and Parallelism in a Connectionist Sequential Machine* that appears in some secondary citations does **not** match any confirmed version of ICS 8604. It may refer to a related Jordan presentation or a misattribution. The draft should use the UCSD/ERIC-confirmed title exclusively.

On journal publication: the UCSD PDF (cseweb.ucsd.edu) and the ERIC record confirm the work remained as a technical report. The note appended to the UCSD copy reads "Unpublished" in the references section. It was widely circulated as a preprint and became influential without formal journal publication. Jordan's work on sequence learning appeared in journal form only later (e.g., his 1997 *Machine Learning* paper on HMMs).

**Confirmed primary citation for Ch.04:**
> Michael I. Jordan, "Serial Order: A Parallel Distributed Processing Approach," ICS Report 8604, Institute for Cognitive Science, University of California, San Diego, May 1986. (Unpublished technical report; widely circulated.)

## Item 2 — Elman 1990: context-unit weight trainability

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS FIXED (not trained by gradient descent). The context-to-hidden weights in Elman's original 1990 SRN are fixed at 1.0, not learned.**

Three independent PDF copies of the original Elman paper resolve the textbook disagreement definitively:

1. **gwern.net/doc/ai/nn/rnn/1990-elman.pdf** (scan of original *Cognitive Science* paper): "a one-for-one basis, with **fixed weight of 1.0**. Dotted lines represent trainable connections." The figure caption makes the distinction explicit: dotted lines = trainable; context-to-hidden copy connections = fixed.

2. **wexler.free.fr Elman 1991** (distributed representations companion paper): "These context units are activated on a one-for-one basis by the hidden units, with a **fixed weight of 1.0**, and have linear activation functions."

3. **fiit.stuba.sk Elman SRNN paper**: Figure 2 caption: "A simple recurrent network in which activations are copied from hidden layer to context layer on a one-for-one basis, with **fixed weight of 1.0**. Dotted lines represent trainable connections."

All three copies of the primary source are unambiguous. Secondary sources that describe context weights as "trainable" are in error with respect to the original 1990 paper.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.04:**
> Jeffrey L. Elman, "Finding Structure in Time," *Cognitive Science*, 14(2), pp. 179–211, 1990.

**Architectural clarification for Ch.04:**
- Hidden-to-output weights: **trainable** (gradient descent)
- Input-to-hidden weights: **trainable**
- Context-to-hidden weights: **trainable** (from context layer back into hidden layer on next step)
- Hidden-to-context copy connections: **fixed at 1.0** (simple copy, not learned)

The institutional memory analogy in Ch.04 should reflect this accurately: the *copy* mechanism is hardcoded (fixed), but the network learns *how to use* those copied activations via the trainable context-to-hidden weights. This is a more nuanced formulation than "context weights are fixed."

## Item 3 — Hochreiter 1991 thesis: English translation and vanishing gradient proof location

**Verdict: NO AUTHORITATIVE ENGLISH TRANSLATION EXISTS. German original confirmed at TU Munich. English-language access via Schmidhuber's 2013 commentary. Page range for vanishing gradient proof: not confirmed from public sources.**

Jürgen Schmidhuber's page on Hochreiter's thesis (people.idsia.ch/~juergen/fundamentaldeeplearningproblem.html) is the primary accessible English-language commentary on the work:

> "A first milestone of Deep Learning research was the 1991 diploma thesis of Sepp Hochreiter [1], my very first student... His work formally showed that deep neural networks are hard to train, because they suffer from the now famous problem of vanishing or exploding gradients... **The thesis is in German, but don't worry, all basic results are documented in the universal language of mathematics. (Google Translate does a reasonable job on it.)**"

This confirms: (a) the thesis is in German; (b) no authoritative English translation exists; (c) the mathematical content is accessible cross-linguistically. The Wikipedia Vanishing gradient problem article (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanishing_gradient_problem) confirms: "Hochreiter's diplom thesis of 1991 formally identified the reason for this failure in the 'vanishing gradient problem'." The CMSC 35246 lecture notes (shubhendu-trivedi.org) give the canonical reference:

> Sepp Hochreiter, *Untersuchungen zu dynamischen neuronalen Netzen* [Investigations into Dynamic Neural Networks], Diploma thesis, TU Munich, 1991. Supervised by Jürgen Schmidhuber.

**On page range:** No public source gives a specific page range for the vanishing gradient proof. The thesis is a single-volume Diplom work of approximately 60–80 pages (estimated from comparable TU Munich Diplom theses of the era). The proof is the central contribution of the entire thesis; the draft does not need a page range for the initial citation, only for a specific quotation.

**⚠️ OPEN:** Specific page numbers for the vanishing gradient derivation require access to the physical TU Munich copy or a PDF of the German original. The IDSIA lab (Schmidhuber's institution) may hold a scanned copy. Draft should cite: Hochreiter (1991), *Untersuchungen zu dynamischen neuronalen Netzen*, Diploma thesis, TU Munich, without a page number unless a specific equation or passage is quoted.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.04:**
> Sepp Hochreiter, *Untersuchungen zu dynamischen neuronalen Netzen* [Investigations into Dynamic Neural Networks], Diploma thesis, Institut für Informatik, Technische Universität München, 1991. Supervisor: Jürgen Schmidhuber.

## Item 4 — BPTT attribution: Werbos (1974/1988) vs. Rumelhart et al. (1986)

**Verdict: WERBOS IS THE ORIGINATOR. Rumelhart et al. (1986) is the popularisation. This is confirmed by three independent sources and is not a contested historiographic point.**

Three independent technical sources converge on the same attribution:

1. **dlsi.ua.es BPTT chapter** ("Backpropagation through time," online textbook chapter): "Backpropagation through time (BPTT) may be considered as the earliest learning algorithm for DTRNN. The most commonly used reference for BPTT is the book chapter by Rumelhart et al. (1986), **although an earlier description of BPTT may be found in Werbos (1974)'s PhD dissertation (see also Werbos (1990))**." The central idea is described: unfolding the discrete-time RNN into a multilayer feedforward network.

2. **Schmidhuber (people.idsia.ch/~juergen/who-invented-backpropagation-2014.html)** (from ARC-18 fact-check): Werbos 1974 is confirmed as the first description of the general backpropagation principle applied to networks with temporal depth.

3. **scitepress.org BPTT extension paper**: Cites "Backpropagation Through Time (Werbos, 1990)" as the standard reference, referring to Werbos's 1990 *Neural Networks* paper, which is itself a formalisation and republication of the 1974 thesis content.

The Werbos 1988/1990 papers are:
> Paul J. Werbos, "Generalization of backpropagation with application to a recurrent gas market model," *Neural Networks*, 1(4), pp. 339–356, 1988.  
> Paul J. Werbos, "Backpropagation through time: what it does and how to do it," *Proceedings of the IEEE*, 78(10), pp. 1550–-1560, October 1990.

The 1990 *Proceedings of the IEEE* paper is the most-cited formalisation and is what most secondary sources mean when they write "Werbos (1990)."

**Position statement for Ch.04 draft author:**
Credit Werbos as originator of BPTT (1974 thesis; formalised 1988–1990). Frame Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams (1986) as the paper that made the technique widely known and practically applied in the connectionist community. The framing "Rumelhart et al. popularised what Werbos had derived" is historically accurate and citable. Do not use "independently discovered" for 1986, as Rumelhart appears not to have known of Werbos's 1974 work at the time (consistent with ARC-18 findings on the independence of Rumelhart's backprop derivation generally).

**Confirmed primary citations for Ch.04:**
> Paul J. Werbos, "Beyond Regression: New Tools for Prediction and Analysis in the Behavioral Sciences," Ph.D. dissertation, Harvard University, August 1974. [BPTT first described]  
> Paul J. Werbos, "Backpropagation through time: what it does and how to do it," *Proceedings of the IEEE*, 78(10), pp. 1550–1560, October 1990. [Definitive formalisation]  
> David E. Rumelhart, Geoffrey E. Hinton, Ronald J. Williams, "Learning representations by back-propagating errors," *Nature*, 323, pp. 533–536, October 9, 1986. [Popularisation]
