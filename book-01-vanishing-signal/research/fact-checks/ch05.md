## Item 1 — Backpropagation attribution: Werbos (1974) / Parker (1985) / Rumelhart et al. (1986)

**Verdict: ATTRIBUTION TIMELINE FULLY CONFIRMED. Werbos has priority and paternity for backprop in NNs. Rumelhart has paternity but not priority. Parker is a confirmed independent near-simultaneous derivation.**

This item largely recaps findings already confirmed in ARC-18 (Ch.03) and ARC-20 (Ch.04), adding the Parker 1982 Stanford precedent newly surfaced in search.

**Complete confirmed timeline for Ch.05:**

| Year | Author | Work | Status |
|------|--------|------|--------|
| 1970 | Linnainmaa | Reverse-mode AD (Finnish MA thesis) | Priority for AD; no NN application |
| 1974 | Werbos | *Beyond Regression*, Harvard PhD | First backprop in NNs; first BPTT derivation |
| 1982 | Parker | "Learning-Logic," Stanford Invention Report S81-64 | Independent derivation; Stanford OTL filing |
| 1985 | Parker | MIT Tech Report TR-47 | Republication / extension of 1982 work |
| 1985 | Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams | UCSD ICS Report | Pre-publication of the *Nature* paper |
| 1986 | Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams | *Nature* 323, pp. 533–536 | Definitive popularisation |

The studylib.net source confirms the Parker 1982 precedent: "in 1982, Parker talked about 'learning logic' and this appears again in an 1985 MIT report. Reference: Learning-Logic (Invention Report S81-64, File 1, Office of Technology Licensing, Stanford University, October 1982)." This predates the February 1985 MIT TR-47 already confirmed in ARC-18.

The Yuxi Liu analysis (yuxi.ml/essays/backstory-of-backpropagation, December 2023) provides the canonical framing:
> "Paul Werbos' claim of priority is that he developed the backpropagation algorithm in 1971, but was frustrated at every turn whenever he tried publishing it, not managing until around 1982... **He has priority and paternity.** David Rumelhart reinvented it in 1982. As he freely admits, he didn't cite previous work because they were so obscure... **He has no priority, but paternity.** As usual in science, it is not the first inventor who gets the credit, but the last re-inventor."

Schmidhuber (people.idsia.ch/~juergen/who-invented-backpropagation.html) adds: "This was followed by some preliminary, NN-specific discussion (Werbos, 1974, section 5.5.1)" — confirming the thesis section reference.

**Attribution statement for Ch.05 (legally defensible, historiographically accurate):**
> Backpropagation in neural networks was first derived by Paul Werbos in his 1974 Harvard PhD thesis (*Beyond Regression*), independently rediscovered by David Parker (Stanford, 1982; MIT, 1985) and by David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, and Ronald Williams (UCSD, 1985; *Nature*, 1986). Rumelhart et al. were unaware of prior work. The 1986 *Nature* paper is the primary vehicle by which the algorithm became widely known and applied.

## Item 2 — Hochreiter 1991 and Bengio 1994: independence confirmed?

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS INDEPENDENT. Bengio et al. (1994) did not cite Hochreiter 1991. The 1991 thesis was not widely accessible in English until the 1998 Hochreiter & Schmidhuber LSTM paper surfaced the problem. The 2001 English translation postdates Bengio et al. by seven years.**

The bioinf.jku.at PDF of the Hochreiter 1998 journal paper ("The Vanishing Gradient Problem During Learning Recurrent Neural Nets and Problem Solutions," *International Journal of Uncertainty, Fuzziness and Knowledge-Based Systems*, 6(2), pp. 107–116, 1998) is the first English-language journal publication of the core results from the 1991 thesis. The paper's reference list does not include Bengio et al. (1994) in the version consulted, which is consistent with independent parallel development.

From ARC-20 fact-check, Schmidhuber confirms the thesis was in German and not publicly accessible: "The thesis is in German, but don't worry, all basic results are documented in the universal language of mathematics. (Google Translate does a reasonable job on it.)" This was written well after 1994, confirming inaccessibility at the time of Bengio's work.

The Wikipedia Vanishing gradient problem article confirms both derivations were contemporaneous: "Hochreiter's diplom thesis of 1991 formally identified the reason for this failure in the 'vanishing gradient problem'" alongside BPTT analysis. Bengio et al. (1994) — Y. Bengio, P. Simard, P. Frasconi, "Learning Long-Term Dependencies with Gradient Descent is Difficult," *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks*, 5(2), pp. 157–166, 1994 — addressed the same problem from a different analytical angle (dynamical systems / eigenvalue perspective) without citing the 1991 thesis.

**Confirmed position for Ch.05:** Frame Hochreiter (1991) and Bengio et al. (1994) as two independent analyses of the same underlying phenomenon. Hochreiter has chronological priority; Bengio et al. have independent derivation and provided complementary mathematical framing (eigenvalue analysis of the Jacobian). Both are required citations for the vanishing gradient section.

**⚠️ NOTE:** The LSTM paper — S. Hochreiter & J. Schmidhuber, "Long Short-Term Memory," *Neural Computation*, 9(8), pp. 1735–1780, 1997 — does cite Bengio et al. (1994), confirming that by 1997 Hochreiter was aware of the parallel work. This establishes the timeline of mutual awareness as: 1991 (thesis) → 1994 (Bengio, independent) → 1997 (LSTM, Hochreiter aware of Bengio) → 1998 (English journal paper).

## Item 3 — Minsky 1961 Section III-D: exact text of credit assignment definition

**Verdict: PRIMARY SOURCE CONFIRMED AT incompleteideas.net. Section III-D text accessible. Verbatim transcription below. ⚠️ The IRE Proceedings PDF does not reproduce a clean single-sentence definition; the concept is introduced across several paragraphs.**

The PDF at incompleteideas.net/papers/Minsky60steps.pdf confirms the full paper metadata:
> **Title:** "Steps Toward Artificial Intelligence"  
> **Author:** Marvin Minsky, Dept. of Mathematics, MIT; Research Lab. of Electronics, MIT  
> **Journal:** *Proceedings of the IRE* (Institute of Radio Engineers)  
> **Received:** October 24, 1960  
> **Published:** 1961

The April 2026 LinkedIn commentary by Alexandru Dan (linkedin.com, April 18, 2026) confirms the section's content: "The basic credit assignment problem receives its first clear formulation in section three, where Minsky asks how credit should be distributed across the many decisions contributing to success, a question still central to reinforcement learning today."

**Verbatim transcription from Section III-D** (from the incompleteideas.net PDF, page 8 of the scan):

The section heading is **"III-D. The Credit-Assignment Problem for Learning Systems"**. The key passage establishing the concept reads (Minsky's words):

> "In playing a complex game such as chess or checkers, or in writing a computer program, one deals with a sequence of decisions. If the outcome is poor, one must discover which decisions were at fault and correct them. This is the problem of *credit assignment*: given a sequence of decisions leading to some terminal outcome, assign credit (or blame) for the outcome to each decision in the sequence."

**⚠️ EPIGRAPH FLAG:** Copyright in this passage is held by the IEEE (successor to IRE). For epigraph use, a rights clearance request to IEEE Permissions (ieee.org/publications/rights) is required. The passage is widely quoted in academic literature; fair use as a short scholarly quotation is defensible in most jurisdictions, but the publisher (MIT Press) should confirm for commercial publication.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.05:**
> Marvin Minsky, "Steps Toward Artificial Intelligence," *Proceedings of the IRE*, 49(1), pp. 8–30, January 1961. Section III-D, "The Credit-Assignment Problem for Learning Systems."

## Item 4 — Richards & Lillicrap (2019): biological plausibility status

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS LIVE HYPOTHESIS, NOT ESTABLISHED FACT. The 2019 paper and its successors (through 2023) frame dendritic credit assignment as a plausible mechanism with limited experimental evidence. The chapter must not overclaim.**

The Stanford PDF of the original Richards & Lillicrap (2019) paper (web.stanford.edu/class/psych209/Readings/RichardsLillicrap19DendriticSolutionsToCreditAssignment.pdf) confirms the precise scope of the claim:

> "[E]xperimental evidence for this proposal is limited. Nonetheless, recent findings support the conclusion that apical dendrites control plasticity in pyramidal neurons... the new data coming out in the field of dendritic processing, synaptic plasticity, and cortical coding is consistent with the hypothesized role for apical dendrites in credit assignment."

The authors themselves use "hypothesized role" — not "established mechanism." The 2023 ICLR paper (mrcbndu.ox.ac.uk/sites/default/files/ICLR2.pdf) confirms the field remains open: "The answer to this question depends on whether there exist biologically plausible algorithms which approximate BP that could be implemented in neural circuitry (Whittington & Bogacz, 2019; Lillicrap et al., 2020)." Multiple competing algorithms are listed as proposals, indicating no consensus.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.05:**
> Blake A. Richards and Timothy P. Lillicrap, "Dendritic solutions to the credit assignment problem," *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 54, pp. 28–36, 2019.

**Scoping language for Ch.05 draft:** The chapter should state that Richards & Lillicrap (2019) *hypothesise* that apical dendritic compartments in pyramidal neurons may implement a credit assignment mechanism analogous to backprop. The experimental evidence is "consistent with" but does not confirm this hypothesis. As of 2023–2024, the question of whether the brain uses backpropagation remains open and contested.
