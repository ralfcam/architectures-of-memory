## Item 1 — Lighthill Report: exact date and SRC response timeline

**Verdict: PUBLICATION DATE CONFIRMED (early 1973 / cited as January 5, 1973). SRC CUT MONTH: NOT CONFIRMED from primary source — flagged as unverifiable from public records.**

The Chilton Computing archive (chilton-computing.org.uk/inf/literature/reports/lighthill_report/overview.htm) states: "The Lighthill Report was published early in 1973." A timeline source (time.graphics) gives the specific date as **January 5, 1973**; however, this source is a user-generated timeline, not an official record. The Wikipedia Lighthill report article gives the year 1973 without a month.

The Wikipedia AI winter article confirms the effect: "The report led to the complete dismantling of AI research in the UK. AI research continued in only a few universities (Edinburgh, Essex and Sussex)." The AI Innovations Unleashed analysis (aiinnovationsunleashed.com, February 2026) states: "It formed the basis for the decision by the British government to end support for AI research in most British universities." The same source identifies Edinburgh and Sussex as the two survivors (with AI winter article also naming Essex).

On the SRC decision month: no source consulted gives the month in which the SRC formally cut funding following the report's publication. The sequence "Lighthill report → SRC decision → lab closures" is confirmed in the correct order (1973 → 1973/74), but month-level precision is **not available from public secondary sources**. The authoritative source would be the SRC (now EPSRC) institutional archives.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.02:**
> James Lighthill, "Artificial Intelligence: A General Survey," Science Research Council, 1973. (Published early 1973; January 5, 1973 per user-generated timeline — treat as approximate pending archival confirmation.)

**⚠️ OPEN:** SRC decision month unconfirmed. Draft should write "following the report's publication in early 1973" rather than specifying a month. Archival query: EPSRC archive or British Library institutional holdings.

**Alvey revival confirmed for sequencing:**
Wikipedia AI winter: "Research would not revive on a large scale until 1983, when Alvey (a research project of the British Government) began to fund AI again from a war chest of £350 million in response to the Japanese Fifth Generation Project." This anchors the UK AI winter as 1973–1983.

## Item 2 — ALPAC 1966: suspension vs. redirection of MT funding

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS EFFECTIVE SUSPENSION (in the US), framed as redirection toward basic research. The distinction is resolvable: the report recommended redirecting funds to basic computational linguistics, but the practical effect was a near-total end to MT funding for ~20 years.**

The ACL Anthology PDF of the Hutchins (1996) paper (aclanthology.org, "ALPAC — the (in)famous report") states: "Its effect was to bring to an end the substantial funding of MT research." This is the key historiographic authority on ALPAC, authored by W. John Hutchins, the leading historian of machine translation.

The Wikipedia ALPAC article (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALPAC) confirms the committee's framing: the report "emphasizing the need for basic research in computational linguistics; this eventually caused the U.S. government to reduce its funding of the topic dramatically." The Pangeanic blog (blog.pangeanic.com/alpac-report), citing the same Hutchins source, states: "Its effect was to bring to an end the substantial funding of MT research in the United States for some twenty years."

The publication date is confirmed: **November 1966** (Hutchins: "publication thirty years ago in November 1966").

**Confirmed citation for Ch.02:**
> Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (ALPAC), *Languages and Machines: Computers in Translation and Linguistics*, National Academy of Sciences / National Research Council, Washington D.C., November 1966. (Report by a committee of seven scientists led by John R. Pierce.)

**Narrative distinction for Ch.02:** The ALPAC report did NOT use the word "suspend." It recommended redirecting resources toward basic research in computational linguistics. However, the operational outcome was a 20-year near-total freeze on MT funding in the US. The draft should honour this distinction: the report's *language* was "redirect"; the *effect* was suspension.

## Item 3 — Minsky's retrospective response to the *Perceptrons* criticism

**Verdict: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. Minsky did respond publicly but not via a formal published retraction. Two citable positions identified; absence of formal retraction confirmed.**

The Wikipedia *Perceptrons* (book) article (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perceptrons_(book)) provides a citable Minsky statement: "Minsky has compared the book to the fictional book *Necronomicon* in H. P. Lovecraft's tales, a book known to many, but read only by a few." The same article notes: "The authors talk in the expanded edition about the criticism of the book that started in the 1980s, with a new wave of research symbolized by the PDP book."

This points to the **1988 expanded edition** of *Perceptrons* (MIT Press, Minsky & Papert) as containing the authors' own response to the criticism. This is the primary citable source for their retrospective position.

The Yuxi Liu analysis (yuxi.ml/essays/posts/perceptron-controversy, 2023) provides characterisation of Minsky's sustained position: "Minsky and Papert were possibly the most consistent critics of the scaling hypothesis, arguing over decades that neural networks cannot scale beyond mere toy problems." This confirms he did **not** issue a retraction but maintained his position while acknowledging the scale of the controversy.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.02:**
> Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert, *Perceptrons: An Introduction to Computational Geometry*, expanded edition, MIT Press, 1988. (The preface/afterword to the expanded edition contains the authors' response to 1980s criticism.)

**⚠️ PARTIALLY OPEN:** The specific page(s) in the 1988 expanded edition where Minsky and Papert address the 1980s criticism are not confirmed from public sources. The Necronomicon comparison appears in secondary sources without a page citation. Draft should cite the 1988 expanded edition preface with a note that page numbers require physical verification.

No formal published retraction, interview statement, or stand-alone essay by Minsky responding specifically to the "*Perceptrons* killed connectionism" charge has been located in public secondary literature. The 1988 expanded edition preface is the closest available citable source.

## Item 4 — DARPA 1974 AI funding cuts: programme name and decision context

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS MANSFIELD AMENDMENT EFFECT. The specific DARPA AI programme was IPTO (Information Processing Techniques Office). No single "decision document" exists; the mechanism was a congressional funding constraint, not a DARPA-internal decision.**

The Good Science Project analysis (goodscienceproject.org, January 2025) provides the authoritative framing: "The Mansfield Amendment was repealed, it succeeded in forcing changes on DARPA beyond making the D in the name no longer silent. [DARPA PMs] were now careful to justify projects with specific military applications in mind." It further confirms the IPTO budget trajectory: "In congressional testimony in 1981, Fossum reported that DARPA funding in constant dollars had fallen steadily from **1970 to 1976**." By 1979, the IPTO budget was at its low point before recovering to $82 million by 1982.

The mkbergman.com analysis (September 2019) names the mechanism precisely: "the US Congress prohibited further open-ended funding via the **Mansfield Amendments in 1969 and 1973**, such that by 1974 AI funding in the US had largely dried up." The Wikipedia AI winter article confirms the same: "In 1973, professor Sir James Lighthill was asked by the UK Parliament..." alongside the DARPA/US context of the Mansfield Amendment effect.

**Key confirmed facts for Ch.02:**
- **Mechanism:** The Mansfield Amendment (1969, reaffirmed 1973) required all DARPA-funded research to demonstrate direct military relevance. Open-ended basic AI research lost its justification.
- **Affected programme:** IPTO (Information Processing Techniques Office) — DARPA's AI/computing arm.
- **Timeline:** Funding fell steadily from 1970 to 1976 in constant dollars; bottom reached ~1979; recovery began 1979–82.
- **No single decision document:** The cuts were a cumulative effect of congressional budget constraints, not a DARPA-internal programme cancellation.

**Confirmed citation for Ch.02:**
> The Mansfield Amendment (Section 203, Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 1970; reaffirmed in the 1973 version) as the proximate legislative mechanism. DARPA's IPTO as the primary affected programme. No single cancellation document exists; cite the Mansfield Amendment statute and DARPA budget trajectory.

**⚠️ NOTE for draft:** Secondary sources consistently cite "1974" as the year AI funding dried up; the underlying mechanism (Mansfield Amendments of 1969 and 1973) is confirmed. The draft should not imply a single decision in 1974 but rather a multi-year squeeze from 1970–1976, with 1974 as the approximate nadir of activity.
