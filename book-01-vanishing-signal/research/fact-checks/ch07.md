## Item 2 — Did Bengio et al. (1994) cite or know about Hochreiter 1991?

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED INDEPENDENT. Bengio et al. (1994) do NOT cite Hochreiter 1991. Independence is confirmed by cross-checking the reference list and by the iro.umontreal.ca/~lisa bibliography, which lists the two works as separate parallel contributions.**

The University of Montreal LISA lab's own long-term dependencies bibliography page (iro.umontreal.ca/~lisa/bib/pub_subject/longterm) lists both the Hochreiter 1991 thesis and the Bengio et al. (1994) IEEE paper separately without any cross-citation. The Bengio group's bibliography for their long-term dependency work includes:

- Bengio, Y., Simard, P., Frasconi, P. (1994). "Learning Long-Term Dependencies with Gradient Descent is Difficult." *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks*, 5(2):157–166.
- Bengio, Y., Frasconi, P. (1994). "An EM approach to learning sequential behavior." Technical Report DSI 11-94, Università di Firenze.
- Bengio, Y., Frasconi, P., Simard, P. (1993). "The problem of learning long-term dependencies in recurrent networks." IEEE ICNN, pp. 1183–1195.

Hochreiter 1991 does not appear anywhere in this bibliography. The LISA page explicitly groups Hochreiter, Bengio, and Frasconi (2001) — a jointly authored retrospective — as a post-hoc synthesis written *after* both groups had become aware of each other's work.

The 1997 LSTM paper confirms the timeline of mutual awareness: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) is the first document where Bengio's 1994 paper and Hochreiter's 1991 thesis appear together in a single reference list. This confirms that cross-awareness was established by 1997, not before.

**Position statement for Ch.07:** The 1994 independence claim is verified. The draft may state without qualification: "Bengio et al. (1994) did not cite Hochreiter (1991); the two analyses were developed independently and reached convergent conclusions through different mathematical formalisms."

## Item 3 — Schmidhuber's residual connection claim (arXiv:2509.24732)

**Verdict: ⚠️ CLAIM IS TRACEABLE TO PRIMARY SOURCE BUT REQUIRES CAREFUL SCOPING. The thesis does introduce a self-connected node with weight 1.0 (identity self-loop) in Chapter 4 (constant error flow node). This is the mathematical mechanism Schmidhuber identifies as a "recurrent residual connection." However, Hochreiter does not use the term "residual connection" — that terminology is retrospective. The claim is technically defensible but is Schmidhuber's framing, not Hochreiter's vocabulary.**

From the bioinf.jku.at primary PDF, Chapter 4.2 ("Linearer KFR-Knoten") introduces the linear constant-error-flow node. The introduction (p. 1) describes the thesis goal as constructing networks with "Speicherknoten" (memory nodes) that can carry information over arbitrarily long time intervals. This is accomplished in Chapter 4 by requiring the self-connection weight of these nodes to be 1.0 — an identity function — so that the error derivative along that path is always 1.0 × 1.0 = 1.0, preventing both vanishing and explosion.

Schmidhuber's arXiv:2509.24732 paper (*Who Invented Deep Residual Learning?*) explicitly frames this as:
> "★ 1991: Sepp Hochreiter solves vanishing gradient problem through recurrent residual connections (weight 1.0)"

And on X/Twitter (SchmidhuberAI, post dated ~Sept 27 2025, retrieved from x.com/SchmidhuberAI/status/1972300268550369631):
> "1991: Sepp Hochreiter introduced residual connections for recurrent NNs (RNNs) in a diploma thesis (June 1991)... His recurrent residual connection was mathematically derived from first principles to overcome the fundamental deep learning problem of vanishing or exploding gradients... a neural unit with the identity activation function has a connection to itself, and the weight of this connection is 1.0."

The themoonlight.io review of arXiv:2509.24732 (published Oct 2025) accurately summarises the paper's argument: the ResNet is characterised as an "open-gated Highway Net" and "feedforward variant of the 1997 LSTM," with the original recurrent residual connection traced to the 1991 thesis.

**Critical distinction for Ch.07 draft:**
1. **What the thesis actually contains (verified):** A self-connected RNN unit with a fixed weight of 1.0, motivated by the need for constant error flow. This is in Chapter 4.
2. **What Schmidhuber claims (retrospective framing):** That this constitutes the invention of residual connections, predating Highway Networks (May 2015) and ResNet (Dec 2015) by 24 years.
3. **What the ML community consensus is:** The Highway Network (Srivastava, Greff, Schmidhuber 2015) and ResNet (He et al. 2015) are standardly credited as introducing residual connections for deep feedforward networks. Schmidhuber's claim is contested and not the majority view in the field.

**⚠️ OPEN FLAG:** The draft must not assert Hochreiter (1991) "invented residual connections" without qualification. The correct formulation is: "Hochreiter's constant-error-flow node (Chapter 4) employed a self-loop with weight 1.0 — a structure that Schmidhuber (2025) has subsequently argued constitutes the first use of residual connections in neural networks. This claim is contested; the prevailing attribution for feedforward residual connections remains He et al. (ResNet, 2015) and Srivastava et al. (Highway Networks, 2015)." Suggested query for further fact-check: `"residual connections origin history Highway Networks ResNet LSTM constant error carousel priority dispute"`

## Item 4 — IDSIA PDF upload date: when did the thesis become publicly available online?

**Verdict: ⚠️ EXACT DATE NOT DETERMINABLE FROM PUBLIC SOURCES. Two PDFs exist at different URLs. The IDSIA/Schmidhuber-hosted PDF (people.idsia.ch) cannot be dated from Wayback Machine without a successful crawl. The bioinf.jku.at PDF (Hochreiter's own institutional page) is separately hosted. Neither PDF carries an embedded creation date in the retrieved content. The "available but ignored" narrative requires qualification.**

Two confirmed PDF locations:
1. **https://people.idsia.ch/~juergen/SeppHochreiter1991ThesisAdvisorSchmidhuber.pdf** — Schmidhuber's IDSIA page. This URL format ("SeppHochreiter1991ThesisAdvisorSchmidhuber") suggests it was uploaded as an advocacy document, not an archival deposit — the naming convention implies post-hoc framing. The Wayback Machine crawl URL attempted (web.archive.org) did not return a dated first-capture result in this session.
2. **https://www.bioinf.jku.at/publications/older/3804.pdf** — Hosted on Hochreiter's JKU Linz institutional page. The path `/publications/older/` implies archival status but does not provide a creation date.

What is known from the IDSIA FKI-207-95 technical report (people.idsia.ch/~juergen/FKI-207-95ocr.pdf — a 1995 Schmidhuber technical report that cites Hochreiter 1991 in its bibliography): the thesis was known and cited within the IDSIA/TU München research group by 1995. This is a different question from *public web availability*.

The broader "available but ignored" narrative depends on the following distinction:
- The thesis was known within its direct lineage (Schmidhuber group) throughout the 1990s.
- Whether it was web-accessible to the broader ML community before the 1997 LSTM paper is unconfirmed. Web archiving of academic PDFs was nascent in 1995–1997.
- The 2001 English translation/chapter in *A Field Guide to Dynamical Recurrent Networks* (IEEE Press) — Hochreiter, Bengio, Frasconi (2001) — is the first widely accessible English-language formulation of the thesis work.

**⚠️ OPEN FLAG:** The draft should not claim the thesis was "publicly available online" before the 1997 LSTM paper without a confirmed first-upload date. The safe formulation is: "The thesis circulated within Schmidhuber's research group and was cited in internal reports from 1995 onward, but broad English-language accessibility arrived only with the 2001 IEEE Press chapter." Suggested query: `"Wayback Machine people.idsia.ch Hochreiter 1991 thesis first archive crawl date 1996 1997 1998"`
