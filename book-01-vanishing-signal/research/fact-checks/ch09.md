## Item 1 — Walsh & Ungson (1991): exact journal, volume, issue, and page range

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED. The paper is published in *Academy of Management Review*, NOT *Administrative Science Quarterly*. Volume 16, Issue 1, pages 57–91, January 1991.**

The issue body tentatively placed the paper in *Administrative Science Quarterly*. This is a common secondary-source error. The primary bibliographic record, confirmed from three independent sources:

1. **IHMC/skat mirror (skat.ihmc.us):** Displays the ABI/INFORM Global header verbatim: *"Academy of Management. The Academy of Management Review; Jan 1991; 16, 1; ABI/INFORM Global — pg. 57"*
2. **Scribd primary document record (scribd.com/document/795141338):** Header reads: *"Walsh, J. P., & Ungson, G. R. (1991). Organizational memory. Academy of Management Review, 16(1), 57–91."*
3. **Warwick OLK5 review paper (warwick.ac.uk):** Cites as "Walsh and Ungson (1991)" throughout; Anderson & Sun (2010, *Management Learning*) published in SAGE Journals confirms the venue as *Academy of Management Review*.

**⚠️ JOURNAL CORRECTION REQUIRED:** Any draft text or citation list that attributes this paper to *Administrative Science Quarterly* must be corrected. The correct journal is **Academy of Management Review**.

**Confirmed citation:**
> J. P. Walsh and G. R. Ungson, "Organizational Memory," *Academy of Management Review*, vol. 16, no. 1, pp. 57–91, January 1991.

**Additional context for Ch.09:** The paper is described by Anderson & Sun (2010) as the most-cited work in organizational memory literature, cited over 300 times. Its key contribution is the five-bin retention model: individuals, culture, transformations, structures, and ecology. The irony for the chapter's thesis — that this taxonomy itself demonstrates how organisations spatially externalise memory into non-neural substrate — is a strong narrative thread.

## Item 2 — Wegner TMS: 1985 hypothesis vs. 1987 published chapter

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED. The correct primary citation is the 1987 published chapter. The 1985 reference is an internal citation within the 1987 chapter to an earlier conference or working paper by Wegner, Giuliano & Hertel, not a standalone publication of the TMS theory.**

From the Scribd primary document of the chapter (scribd.com/document/1009096373), the first page of Wegner's chapter reads:

> "B. Mullen et al. (eds.), *Theories of Group Behavior* © Springer-Verlag New York Inc. 1987 — 186 — Daniel M. Wegner — *A transactive memory system is a set of individual memory systems in combination with the communication that takes place between individuals (Wegner, Giuliano, & Hertel, 1985).*"

This makes the publication structure clear:

| Reference | What it is | Status |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Wegner, Giuliano & Hertel (1985) | Internal citation in the 1987 chapter, cited as the initial articulation of the TMS concept | Likely a conference paper or working note; no standalone published record confirmed |
| Wegner (1987) | Published chapter: "Transactive Memory: A Contemporary Analysis of the Group Mind" | **The primary citable source** |

The Carlson School of Management TMS review (Argote & Ren, 2012, *Journal of Management Studies*) — a highly authoritative secondary source — cites:

> Wegner, D. M. (1987). 'Transactive memory: a contemporary analysis of the group mind'. In Mullen, B. and Goethals, G. R. (Eds), *Theories of Group Behavior*. New York: Springer-Verlag, Vol. 9. 185–208.

This confirms page range: **185–208**. Volume 9 of the Mullen & Goethals edited series.

The get-alfred.ai TMS overview (2026) also states: *"Daniel Wegner introduced transactive memory systems in 1987: the distributed cognitive system where team members specialize in different knowledge domains."* This is consistent with all primary and authoritative secondary sources.

**Attribution statement for Ch.09:**
> The correct primary citation for TMS is: D. M. Wegner, "Transactive Memory: A Contemporary Analysis of the Group Mind," in B. Mullen and G. R. Goethals (Eds.), *Theories of Group Behavior*, Springer-Verlag, New York, 1987, pp. 185–208.
> The 1985 Wegner, Giuliano & Hertel reference appears only as an internal citation within the 1987 chapter and is not independently published. Do not cite "Wegner, 1985" as a standalone source.

**⚠️ OPEN FLAG:** The 1985 Wegner, Giuliano & Hertel reference is cited in the 1987 chapter but no publication record has been identified. It may be an unpublished manuscript, working paper, or conference presentation. Suggested query: `"Wegner Giuliano Hertel 1985 transactive memory working paper conference unpublished manuscript source"`

## Item 3 — Argote & Ingram (2000) manufacturing shift study: McGrath & Argote primary source

**Verdict: ✅ RESOLVED. The manufacturing shift productivity example in Argote & Ingram (2000) is attributed to Epple, Argote & Murphy (1996), NOT to McGrath & Argote. The McGrath & Argote reference is used for a knowledge reservoirs *framework*, not the productivity example.**

This is a critical distinction. The Studocu full-text extract of Argote & Ingram (2000) reads verbatim:

> *"A framework of knowledge reservoirs (repositories where knowledge is embedded in organizations) that was developed by McGrath and Argote (in press) is used to demonstrate why knowledge transfer can be difficult..."*

Then, on the manufacturing shift study:

> *"One study found that the knowledge embedded in the task–tool network of a plant transferred quickly and effectively to new members (Epple, Argote, & Murphy, 1996). The study analyzed the productivity of a manufacturing plant that added a second shift almost 2 years after operating with one shift. The second shift, composed primarily of members who were new to the organization, used the same tools and task sequences embedded in the assembly line that the first shift had used. The second shift achieved a level of productivity in a couple of weeks that the first shift took many months to achieve.*"

The primary empirical source for the shift productivity example is therefore:

> **Epple, D., Argote, L., & Murphy, K. (1996).** "An empirical investigation of the micro structure of knowledge acquisition and transfer through learning by doing." *Operations Research*, 44(1), 77–86.

McGrath & Argote (in press at time of 2000 publication) is cited only for the reservoirs framework (members, tools, tasks). The subsequently published form of this framework is:

> **McGrath, J. E., & Argote, L. (2001).** "Group processes in organizational contexts." In M. A. Hogg & R. S. Tindale (Eds.), *Blackwell Handbook of Social Psychology: Group Processes*. Blackwell, pp. 603–627.

**Attribution statement for Ch.09:**
> The second-shift productivity example should cite Epple, Argote & Murphy (1996, *Operations Research* 44(1):77–86) as the primary empirical source. McGrath & Argote (2001) is the correct published form of the knowledge reservoirs framework used to organise the Argote & Ingram (2000) argument. Argote & Ingram itself is published in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, vol. 82, issue 1, pp. 150–169, 2000 (confirmed via EconPapers/REPEC and ScienceDirect).

**Confirmed citations:**
> L. Argote and P. Ingram, "Knowledge Transfer: A Basis for Competitive Advantage in Firms," *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, vol. 82, no. 1, pp. 150–169, 2000.
> D. Epple, L. Argote, and K. Murphy, "An empirical investigation of the micro structure of knowledge acquisition and transfer through learning by doing," *Operations Research*, vol. 44, no. 1, pp. 77–86, 1996.

## Item 4 — Nonaka SECI: 1994 Organization Science article vs. 1995 Knowledge-Creating Company

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED. The full four-mode SECI spiral was introduced in the 1994 Organization Science article. The 1995 book is the popular elaboration and application. Cite 1994 as the primary source for the theoretical model.**

From the Dauphine-hosted primary PDF of the 1994 article (lamsade.dauphine.fr confirmed):

- **Full citation:** Ikujiro Nonaka, "A Dynamic Theory of Organizational Knowledge Creation," *Organization Science*, Vol. 5, No. 1, pp. 14–37, February 1994. Published by INFORMS.
- **JSTOR stable URL:** http://links.jstor.org/sici?sici=1047-7039%28199402%295%3A1%3C14%3AADTOOK%3E2.0.CO%3B2-G

The 1994 article contains:
1. **Figure 1** — "Modes of the Knowledge Creation" — the complete 2×2 SECI matrix (Socialization / Externalization / Combination / Internalization)
2. **Figure 2** — "Spiral of Organizational Knowledge Creation" — the full ontological/epistemological spiral
3. The complete theoretical exposition of all four modes with definitions (pp. 18–20)
4. The Modal Shift and Spiral of Knowledge section (pp. 20–21)

The 1994 article's abstract confirms:
> *"four patterns of interaction involving tacit and explicit knowledge are identified... A theoretical framework is developed which provides an analytical perspective on the constituent dimensions of knowledge creation."*

The Frontiers in Psychology 2019 paper (PMC6914727) — a peer-reviewed source — cites "Nonaka (1994)" for SECI throughout and explicitly states: *"The SECI model (Nonaka, 1994) is the best-known conceptual framework for understanding knowledge generation processes in organizations."* It cites the 1994 article, not the 1995 book, as the theoretical primary source.

The 1995 book (Nonaka, I. & Takeuchi, H., *The Knowledge-Creating Company*, Oxford University Press, 1995) operationalises the model with extensive case studies of Japanese firms (Honda, Canon, NEC, etc.) but does not introduce the four SECI modes — these were already fully defined in 1994.

**Attribution statement for Ch.09:**
> Cite Nonaka (1994) as the primary source for the SECI model and the four-mode spiral. The 1995 OUP book may be cited for empirical case material and popularisation. Using only Nonaka & Takeuchi (1995) without citing the 1994 article misattributes the theoretical origin by one year and to a co-authored book rather than the sole-authored journal article where the formal framework was published.

**Confirmed citation:**
> I. Nonaka, "A Dynamic Theory of Organizational Knowledge Creation," *Organization Science*, vol. 5, no. 1, pp. 14–37, February 1994.
> I. Nonaka and H. Takeuchi, *The Knowledge-Creating Company: How Japanese Companies Create the Dynamics of Innovation*, Oxford University Press, New York, 1995.
