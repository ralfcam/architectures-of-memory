### 2. Army AAR doctrine — FM 25-20 superseded status and current authoritative source

FM 25-20 (1993), *A Leader’s Guide to After-Action Reviews*, was the defining doctrinal source for the US Army AAR method for over two decades. It has been **superseded** by two subsequent publications:

**TC 7-0.1, *After Action Reviews*** — This Training Circular, available via the Central Army Registry (CAR) at rdl.train.army.mil, is the current Army doctrinal reference specifically dedicated to AAR process and procedures. Its opening states: “TC 7-0.1, After Action Reviews, provides Army leaders the fundamental processes and procedures to plan and conduct after action reviews (AAR).” It supersedes the Leader’s Guide to After-Action Reviews dated August 2012, which itself superseded FM 25-20 (1993).

**FM 7-0, Appendix K** (in force as of 2023, available via First Army portal) also contains an authoritative AAR definition and procedural guidance: “An after action review (AAR) is a guided analysis of a Soldier’s or organization’s performance, conducted at appropriate times during and at the conclusion of a training event or operation.”

**Note on ATP 5-19:** The issue body proposed ATP 5-19 as the superseding document. This is **incorrect**. ATP 5-19 (*Risk Management*, April 2014, with Change 1 September 2014) supersedes **FM 5-19** (Risk Management, 2006), not FM 25-20. ATP 5-19 does reference AARs as a tool within the risk management cycle but is not the primary AAR doctrinal source. The correct superseding publications for FM 25-20 (AAR doctrine) are **TC 7-0.1** (primary, current) and **FM 7-0 Appendix K**.

**Verdict for Ch.12:** Replace the FM 25-20 citation with TC 7-0.1 (*After Action Reviews*, current edition, US Army Combined Arms Center) as the authoritative primary source for AAR doctrine. ATP 5-19 should not be substituted — it addresses risk management, not AAR procedure. The issue body’s proposed substitution of ATP 5-19 for FM 25-20 is itself an error that must not propagate into the chapter.

### 3. Argyris defensive routines — primary source identification

The specific claim under scrutiny is: *defensive routines short-circuit double-loop learning*. The attribution must be traced to the correct primary source.

**Publication timeline for Argyris on defensive routines:**

| Year | Work | Publisher | Relevance |
|------|------|-----------|----------|
| 1974 | *Theory in Practice* (with Schön) | Jossey-Bass | Introduces theories of action, Model I / Model II; does **not** use the term “defensive routines” |
| 1978 | *Organizational Learning* (with Schön) | Addison-Wesley | Develops single-loop / double-loop learning; defensive routines referenced but not the central construct |
| 1982 | *Reasoning, Learning, and Action* | Jossey-Bass | Develops Model I / Model II in depth; defensive routines present but not the titular concept |
| **1985** | ***Strategy, Change and Defensive Routines*** | **Pitman Publishing** | **First work with “defensive routines” as the central, titular concept; 368 pages; “Defensive routines are anti-learning.”** |
| 1990 | *Overcoming Organizational Defenses* | Allyn & Bacon | Full treatment of defensive routines in organizational context; widely cited as the accessible synthesis |
| 1993 | *Knowledge for Action* | Jossey-Bass | Practical guide to overcoming barriers; extends 1990 framework |

**Primary source for the specific claim (defensive routines short-circuit double-loop learning):**

The **definitional** statement of defensive routines as organisational phenomena is found in **Argyris, C. (1985). *Strategy, Change and Defensive Routines*. Pitman Publishing** — this is the work where “Defensive routines are anti-learning” appears as a direct formulation. Google Scholar citation count: 1,854, confirming it as a standard academic reference.

The **most widely cited** and accessible synthesis, and the source for the canonical definition “Organizational defensive routines are actions or policies that prevent individuals or segments of the organization from experiencing embarrassment or threat”, is **Argyris, C. (1990). *Overcoming Organizational Defenses: Facilitating Organizational Learning*. Allyn & Bacon / Prentice-Hall** (Google Scholar citation count: 5,200). The infed.org analysis of Argyris confirms that the connection between Model I, defensive routines, and the obstruction of double-loop learning is most fully elaborated in the 1990 work and its successors.

**Verdict for Ch.12:** For the claim that defensive routines short-circuit double-loop learning:
- **Cite Argyris (1985)** as the primary source where defensive routines are first made the central analytical object with explicit anti-learning framing.
- **Cite Argyris (1990)** as the source for the canonical organisational definition and the fullest treatment linking defensive routines to the obstruction of double-loop learning at the organisational level.
- Do **not** cite *Theory in Practice* (1974) or *Organizational Learning* (1978) as primary sources for defensive routines — those works are primary sources for single-loop / double-loop learning and theories of action, respectively, not for defensive routines as the central concept.
- Do **not** cite *Reasoning, Learning, and Action* (1982) as the defensive-routines primary source; that work is correctly cited for the elaboration of Model I / Model II action science.

### 4. NASA institutional reform — Challenger (1986) vs. Columbia (2003) as distinct triggers

The two disasters triggered **distinct** institutional reform waves with different organisational foci. The chapter must not conflate them.

**Challenger (January 28, 1986) → PPMI reform wave — focus: individual competence and formal training:**

NASA’s own APPEL Knowledge Services history page states explicitly: “APPEL Knowledge Services traces its roots to 1988 with the launch of the **Program and Project Management Initiative (PPMI)**, established in response to the Space Shuttle Challenger disaster.” The PPMI aimed to strengthen NASA’s workforce through formal training, on-the-job learning, and lessons-learned sharing. The PMI white paper (*NASA’s PMO: Building and Sustaining a Learning Organization*) confirms: “After Challenger, the focus was on **building individual competence**.” A former Apollo director, retired USAF General Samuel C. Phillips, led a NASA Management Study Group whose report cited the need to “strengthen agency-wide leadership in developing and managing people” — resulting in the establishment of the **Program and Project Initiative (PPI) in 1989**. Training was traditional: classroom learning and on-the-job training.

**Columbia (February 1, 2003) → CAIB reform wave — focus: organisational learning culture and knowledge sharing:**

The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (CAIB) report concluded that “NASA’s current organization…has not demonstrated the characteristics of a learning organization” and cited the failure to communicate critical safety information across the organisation. The CAIB found that institutional practices from the Challenger era — including inadequate concern over deviations, a silent safety programme, and schedule pressure — had **returned to NASA by 2003**, demonstrating that the PPMI individual-competence reforms had not produced systemic organisational learning. After Columbia, NASA’s response was to embed knowledge capture and sharing into its organisational DNA: the PMI white paper states, “After Columbia, NASA realized it had to become a learning organisation.” This drove the evolution of PPMI into what is now APPEL Knowledge Services, with its systematic lessons-learned databases and knowledge-sharing infrastructure.

**Verdict for Ch.12:** The two reform waves are clearly distinguished:
- **Challenger (1986) → PPMI (1988/1989):** Triggered by individual and management competence gaps; response was formal training programs for project managers — a first-order, single-loop institutional response.
- **Columbia (2003) → CAIB → APPEL evolution:** Triggered by the failure of the Challenger-era reforms to produce genuine organisational learning; response was a systemic cultural and knowledge-architecture transformation — a second-order, double-loop institutional response.

This distinction is analytically critical for Ch.12: it illustrates precisely the Argyris distinction between single-loop (Challenger → PPMI) and double-loop (Columbia → APPEL) organisational learning at the institutional scale.

3. **Argyris’s own ladder:** The 1974→1978→1985→1990 publication sequence shows Argyris progressively theorising the obstacle to his own prescriptions — each book is a meta-learning event about why the previous book’s prescriptions failed.
4. **NASA as the controlled experiment:** Challenger and Columbia are a 17-year natural experiment in the difference between single-loop and double-loop institutional reform, with the CAIB report as the evaluation instrument — the strongest empirical case in the organisational-learning literature.
5. **The return of the repressed:** The CAIB finding that Challenger-era dysfunctions had “returned” by 2003 is a direct instantiation of Argyris’s defensive routines at institutional scale — the chapter can close this loop explicitly.
