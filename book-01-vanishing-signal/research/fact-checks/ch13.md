## Findings

### 1. DK / Marcovich / Kahn fragment numbering — concordance for B12, B49a, B91

Three independent numbering systems are in active use in Heraclitus scholarship. The chapter must commit to one and cross-reference the others in a footnote.

**Diels–Kranz (DK) system:** The standard in anglophone and international scholarship. Heraclitus is chapter 22 of the *Fragmente der Vorsokratiker* (1st ed. 1903; definitive 5th ed. 1934–7, Diels rev. Kranz; 6th ed. 1952). Fragments are designated 22B + number. The three river fragments are **B12, B49a, B91**. This is the system used by the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Presocratics Reader (McKirahan), and most anglophone commentaries.

**Marcovich system (1967):** Miroslav Marcovich, *Heraclitus: Greek Text with a Short Commentary* (Merida, 1967; 2nd ed. Sankt Augustin, 2001). Uses his own fragment ordering; the three river fragments are:
- DK B12 = **Marcovich frg. 40**
- DK B49a = **Marcovich frg. 40c2**
- DK B91 = **Marcovich frg. 40c3**

This is confirmed by the Fronterotta (2014) article in the Gale Academic database, which states: “These are the three fragments which appear in the collection of Diels and Kranz under the numbers 12, 49a and 91, and correspond, in the edition of Marcovich to the numbers 40, 40c2 and 40c3.” Marcovich treats 49a and 91 as “mere reminiscences” of B12 (the only fragment he accepts as fully authentic), hence his subordiate numbering (40c2, 40c3). This is a **substantive editorial position**, not merely a renumbering: Marcovich believes 49a and 91 are secondary variations, not independent fragments.

**Kahn system (1979):** Charles H. Kahn, *The Art and Thought of Heraclitus* (Cambridge, 1979), presents fragments in his own thematic ordering. His fragment numbers are entirely independent of DK. The heraclitusfragments.com site (a scholarly companion tool) navigates both Kahn numbers and DK cross-references in parallel. The McGill Classics paper on the river fragments refers to B12 as “B2” in Kahn’s numbering (confirming the divergence). The appendix to Kahn’s paperback provides a DK–Kahn concordance (confirmed by the mcrowe.org.uk companion document: “The relationship of the ordering given here with other translations is shown in the appendix”).

**Concordance table for the three river fragments:**

| Concept | DK (standard) | Marcovich (1967) | Kahn (1979) |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| "same rivers / fresh waters ever flowing" | B12 | frg. 40 | XLIX (49) approx. |
| "you cannot step into the same rivers twice" | B49a | frg. 40c2 | variant/secondary |
| "it is not possible to step twice into the same river" | B91 | frg. 40c3 | variant/secondary |

> ⚠️ Kahn’s precise fragment numbers for B12, B49a, B91 require verification against the Kahn appendix concordance. The exact Kahn numbers for B49a and B91 are not confirmed with sufficient precision in this research cycle — flagged as residual open question.

**Verdict for Ch.13:** Use **DK numbering** as the primary system (the international standard). Include a single footnote stating: “Marcovich’s edition (1967, 2nd ed. 2001) numbers these frg. 40, 40c2, and 40c3 respectively, and treats 49a and 91 as secondary reminiscences of B12 rather than independent fragments. Kahn (1979) provides a DK concordance in his appendix.” This resolves the attribution dispute transparently without requiring the chapter body to adjudicate it.

### 2. *Panta rhei* — attribution status: direct fragment or later coinage

**Verdict: *Panta rhei* is NOT an authenticated Heraclitean fragment. It is a post-Heraclitean summary formula, first attested in Simplicius (6th century CE).**

This is confirmed by multiple independent primary and scholarly sources:

**Wikipedia (*Panta rhei* doctrine article):** “Plato attributes this teaching to Heraclitus in his dialogue *Cratylus*, but **the formula first appears in the work of the late antique Neoplatonist Simplicius**.”

**Wikiquote (Heraclitus):** “This statement occurs in Simplicius’ *Commentary on Aristotle’s Physics*, 1313.11; while some sources attribute to Simplicius the coining of the specific phrase ‘πάντα ῥεῖ (*panta rhei*)’ to characterise the concept in the philosophy of Heraclitus, the essential phrasing ‘everything changes’ and variations on it... was current in both Plato and Aristotle’s writings.”

**TheCollector.com (scholarly summary):** “John Burnett, the author of *The Early Greek Philosophy* (1892), believed that ‘Panta Rhei’... perfectly encapsulated the Heraclitean philosophy of flux. However, Burnett also believed that Heraclitus himself **never used this phrase himself**. Kostas Axelos, another modern scholar who studied Heraclitus in-depth, agreed with Burnett. In fact, **there is no ancient source claiming that Heraclitus ever said Panta Rhei**.”

**Transmission chain:** The concept of universal flux is Plato’s attribution in *Cratylus* 402a (early 4th century BCE), where Plato paraphrases Heraclitus as saying “everything gives way and nothing stands fast” (πάντα χωρεῖ καὶ οὐδὲν μένει) and compares it to a river. The compact Greek formula πάντα ῥεῖ appears first in Simplicius, *In Aristotelis Physicorum Libros Commentaria*, 1313.11 (6th century CE) — more than 1,000 years after Heraclitus. Theophrastus (4th–3rd century BCE) transmitted accounts of Heraclitean flux doctrine but did not use the *panta rhei* formula.

**Scholarly consensus:** No serious Heraclitus scholar attributes *panta rhei* as a verbatim fragment. It is described as a “summary formula” or “aphorism which describes Heraclitus’ doctrine” (Wikipedia). The three authenticated river fragments (DK B12, B49a, B91) are the textual basis for the flux doctrine; *panta rhei* is the post-Heraclitean shorthand.

**Verdict for Ch.13:** The chapter must not present *panta rhei* in quotation marks as a Heraclitean saying. It may be used as a conventional summary label with explicit attribution: “the formula *panta rhei* (‘everything flows’), first attested in Simplicius (*In Phys.* 1313.11, 6th c. CE), is the conventional shorthand for the flux doctrine that Plato attributes to Heraclitus in *Cratylus* 402a.” The three DK fragments (especially B12) must be the textual anchor.

The chapter may cite either the paperback or hardback ISBN; the paperback (0-521-28645-X) is the most widely cited in secondary literature. No revised edition exists; the 1979 citation is current and correct.

### 4. Plato *Cratylus* 402a — authoritative translation and Stephanus citation

**Standard reference:** *Cratylus* 402a (Stephanus pagination), specifically **402a8–10**.

**Greek text of the key passage (402a8–10):**
> Λέγει που Ἡράκλειτος ὅτι πάντα χωρεῖ καὶ οὐδὲν μένει, καὶ ποταμοῦ ῡοῇ ἀπεικάζων τὰ ὄντα λέγει ὡς δὶς εἰς τὸν αὐτὸν ποταμὸν οὐκ ἂν ἐμβαίης.

**Authoritative English translations:**

**Fowler (Loeb Classical Library, 1926)** — the standard scholarly edition for Platonic dialogues in English for most of the 20th century:
> “Heraclitus says somewhere that all things are in process and nothing stays still, and likening existing things to the stream of a river, he says you could not step into the same river twice.”  
> — Plato, *Cratylus*, trans. Harold N. Fowler, Loeb Classical Library Vol. 167 (London: Heinemann; Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1926), 402a.

The Cambridge Review confirms the Fowler Loeb as Vol. VI: *Cratylus, Parmenides, Greater Hippias, Lesser Hippias*, 1926 (web:799).

**Books.openedition.org (scholarly edition)** gives the Greek at 402a8–10 as: “πάντα χωρεῖ καὶ οὐδὲν μένει” (“everything gives way and nothing stands fast”) plus the river metaphor.

**Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy** (*Heraclitus*, Barnes’ translation cited):
> “Heraclitus, I believe, says that all things pass and nothing stays, and comparing existing things to the flow of a river, he says you could not step twice into the same river.”  
> — Plato, *Cratylus* 402a = DK 22A6.

**McGill Classics (2008) reading** (web:249): “In what is likely a reference to fragment B12, Plato claims that Heraclitus wrote that we cannot step into the same river twice. Plato deduces from this that Heraclitus believed that all things are constantly changing (Pl. Crat. 402a).”

**Key observation — Plato misquotes Heraclitus:** DK B12 says “you cannot step into the same rivers; for fresh waters are ever flowing in” (a claim about the identity of rivers, not the impossibility of repetition). Plato’s paraphrase at 402a converts this into “you could not step into the same river twice” — which is closer to DK B91. The *Cratylus* passage is Plato’s interpretation, not a direct quotation of Heraclitus, and it conflates the river fragments into a flux doctrine that Heraclitus scholars argue is Platonic rather than strictly Heraclitean. The chapter must be clear that 402a is Plato’s attribution, not Heraclitus’s own words.

**Verdict for Ch.13:** Cite *Cratylus* 402a with Fowler (Loeb, 1926) as the standard English translation, noting the Stephanus reference 402a8–10 for precision. Acknowledge explicitly that this is Plato’s paraphrase of Heraclitus, not a DK fragment, and cross-reference DK B12 as the underlying text Plato is interpreting. The chapter must not present the Fowler translation of 402a as a quotation from Heraclitus.

## Narrative threads for Ch.13

1. **The telephone game at 2,500 years:** B12 (Heraclitus, c. 500 BCE) → 402a (Plato’s paraphrase, c. 360 BCE) → *panta rhei* (Simplicius’ formula, c. 530 CE) → modern quotation books — the entire drift is a textbook case of organisational memory degradation applied to philosophical transmission.
2. **Marcovich’s editorial knife:** Treating B49a and B91 as secondary variations of B12 is itself an act of institutional memory management — deciding which voices in the archive are “original” and which are “mere reminiscences.”
3. **Kahn’s reordering as interpretation:** Kahn’s thematic ordering of the fragments is not neutral; it embeds an interpretive claim about Heraclitean structure. Choosing DK over Kahn is itself a hermeneutic act the chapter can acknowledge.
4. **Plato as unreliable narrator:** The *Cratylus* is the primary ancient source for the flux doctrine, but Plato uses Heraclitus to argue against him. The chapter can exploit the irony that our best “source” for Heraclitus’s flux doctrine is an author who thought it was wrong.
5. **The formula that outlived the fragments:** *Panta rhei* has circulated for 1,000+ years more widely than any authenticated fragment — a memorial that has completely displaced its source. This is the book’s central theme enacted at micro-scale.
