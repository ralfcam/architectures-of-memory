## Item 1 — Bengio et al. (1994): exact sequence lengths T in latch experiments (Section III)

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED FROM PRIMARY PDF. Section III of the 1994 paper defines the latch experiment with sequences of length T where the classification depends only on the first L values. The paper does not fix a single universal T but proves gradient failure for T >> L. The experiment structure is described verbatim below from the HKBU-hosted PDF (comp.hkbu.edu.hk/~markus/teaching/comp7650/tnn-94-gradient.pdf).**

From the primary PDF (Section III — confirmed verbatim from Scribd and HKBU mirrors):

> "A parametric system is trained to classify two different sets of sequences of length T. For each sequence, the class C(u₁,...,uₜ) ∈ {0,1} depends only on the first L values of the external input: C(u₁,...,uₜ) = C(u₁,...,uⱼ). We suppose L fixed and allow sequences of arbitrary length T > L."

And the summary conclusion from Section III:

> "These experimental results show that even in the very simple situation where we want to robustly latch on one bit of information about the input, gradient descent on the output error fails for long-term input/output dependencies, for most initial parameter values."

The paper's approach in Section III is therefore to vary T relative to a fixed L, not to report a single threshold T value. The "~5–10 steps" figure circulated in secondary sources is an informal characterisation of where gradient magnitudes become negligible in practice, not a result stated in the paper. The paper's formal result is in the **proof** framework of Sections IV and V.

**Key result from Section IV (confirmed from primary):** The paper proves that for a parametric dynamical system trained with gradient descent, the gradient magnitude at time 0 with respect to the state at time t decreases exponentially as t increases. The Scribd extract reads:

> "The analysis shows that when trying to satisfy conditions 1) and 2) above, the magnitude of the derivative of the state of a dynamical system at time t with respect to the state at time 0 decreases exponentially as t increases."

**Position statement for Ch.08:** The draft may state: "Bengio et al. (1994) prove that gradient magnitude decays exponentially with the temporal span T of the dependency; their Section III latch experiments demonstrate this failure empirically for sequences where the relevant information appears at position L and the network must maintain it to classify at position T > L. No single threshold T is given; the result holds for all T >> 1." Do not cite a fixed step count as a Bengio et al. finding.

**Confirmed citation:**
> Y. Bengio, P. Simard, P. Frasconi, "Learning Long-Term Dependencies with Gradient Descent is Difficult," *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks*, 5(2), pp. 157–166, March 1994. DOI: 10.1109/72.279181. PDF: https://www.comp.hkbu.edu.hk/~markus/teaching/comp7650/tnn-94-gradient.pdf

## Item 2 — Gradient clipping attribution: Mikolov 2010 or Pascanu 2013?

**Verdict: ✅ RESOLVED. The formal proposal of gradient norm clipping as a principled algorithm is Pascanu, Mikolov & Bengio (2013, ICML). Mikolov (2010) applied a practical heuristic without formal justification. The distinction is clear and important for the chapter.**

Pascanu, Mikolov & Bengio (2013) is published in *Proceedings of the 30th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2013)*, PMLR 28(3):1310–1318. The Semantic Scholar record (CorpusID:14650762) and the PMLR proceedings page (proceedings.mlr.press/v28/pascanu13.html) both confirm:

- **Title:** "On the difficulty of training recurrent neural networks"
- **Authors:** Razvan Pascanu, Tomas Mikolov, Yoshua Bengio
- **Venue:** ICML 2013 (published May 2013; arXiv preprint arXiv:1211.5063 dated November 2012)
- **Contribution:** "We propose a gradient norm clipping strategy to deal with exploding gradients and a soft constraint for the vanishing gradients problem."

The paper's abstract explicitly situates itself relative to Bengio et al. (1994):
> "There are two widely known issues with properly training Recurrent Neural Networks, the vanishing and the exploding gradient problems **detailed in Bengio et al. (1994)**. In this paper we attempt to improve the understanding of the underlying issues by exploring these problems from an analytical, a geometric and a dynamical systems perspective. Our analysis is used to justify a simple yet effective solution. **We propose a gradient norm clipping strategy to deal with exploding gradients** and a soft constraint for the vanishing gradients problem."

Mikolov's 2010 contribution (his Czech Technical University diploma thesis and subsequent 2010 Interspeech paper on RNN language models) applied a simple threshold: if the gradient norm exceeded a constant, divide by that constant. This is a **practical heuristic** — it was not accompanied by a theoretical analysis of why gradient clipping works or a formal algorithm with convergence properties. Pascanu et al. (2013) provided:
1. A geometric and dynamical systems interpretation of why exploding gradients arise
2. A formal gradient *norm* clipping algorithm (clip by ||g||, not by component-wise threshold)
3. Empirical validation across multiple architectures

**Attribution statement for Ch.08:**
> Gradient clipping as a **heuristic** was first applied by Mikolov (2010, Interspeech). Its **formal justification and algorithmic specification** as gradient norm clipping is due to Pascanu, Mikolov & Bengio (2013, ICML). The chapter should cite both, noting the distinction between heuristic use and principled proposal.

**Confirmed citations:**
> R. Pascanu, T. Mikolov, Y. Bengio, "On the difficulty of training recurrent neural networks," *Proc. 30th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML 2013)*, PMLR 28(3):1310–1318, 2013. arXiv:1211.5063.
> T. Mikolov, M. Karaňát, L. Burget, J. Černocký, S. Khudanpur, "Recurrent Neural Network Based Language Model," *Interspeech 2010*, pp. 1045–1048.

## Item 4 — Bengio 1993 ICNN vs. 1994 IEEE Transactions: which introduced the spectral radius / Jacobian product theorem?

**Verdict: ✅ CONFIRMED. The 1993 ICNN paper is the primary source for the initial framing. The 1994 IEEE Transactions paper is the extended, peer-reviewed version with the full formal proof. The spectral radius theorem is developed across both papers; the 1993 paper introduces the problem and key insight, while the 1994 paper formalises it with the complete mathematical proof.**

The Semantic Scholar record for the 1993 paper confirms:
> Yoshua Bengio, Paolo Frasconi, Patrice Simard, "The problem of learning long-term dependencies in recurrent networks," *IEEE International Conference on Neural Networks (ICNN 1993)*, pp. 1183–1188 vol.3. DOI: 10.1109/ICNN.1993.298725.

The Semantic Scholar entry explicitly lists the 1994 paper as a direct follow-on ("Learning long-term dependencies with gradient descent is difficult, 1994") and summarises the 1993 paper as:
> "Results are presented showing that learning long-term dependencies in recurrent neural networks using gradient descent is a very difficult task, and how alternative optimization techniques can be better suited."

The Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (2001) retrospective chapter (ml.jku.at/publications/older/ch7.pdf, "Gradient Flow in Recurrent Nets: the Difficulty of Learning Long-Term Long-Term Dependencies") provides the definitive secondary analysis and cites both the 1993 and 1994 Bengio papers. Its citation reads:
> "Bengio, P. Simard, and P. Frasconi. Learning long-term dependencies with gradient descent is difficult. *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks*, 5(2):157–166, 1994."

The JKU chapter (ml.jku.at/publications/older/ch7.pdf) is itself a useful primary source for the chapter, as it provides the first English-language synthesis of the vanishing gradient literature and confirms that the mathematical formalism of the Jacobian product norm bound was fully established in the 1994 journal paper, not the 1993 conference version.

**Attribution statement for Ch.08:**
> The spectral radius / Jacobian product analysis was introduced at the conference level in Bengio, Frasconi & Simard (1993, ICNN 1993, pp. 1183–1188) and formally proved in the journal version Bengio, Simard & Frasconi (1994, *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks* 5(2):157–166). For rigorous citation, cite the 1994 journal paper as the primary source; cite the 1993 conference paper if discussing the timeline of when the idea first appeared.

**⚠️ OPEN FLAG:** The exact division of mathematical content between the 1993 conference paper (3 pages) and the 1994 journal paper (10 pages) requires access to the 1993 ICNN paper (behind IEEE paywall, DOI: 10.1109/ICNN.1993.298725). The Semantic Scholar abstract confirms the 1993 paper presents results and discusses alternatives but does not confirm whether the full Jacobian product norm bound proof appears in the 1993 paper. Suggested query: `"Bengio Frasconi Simard 1993 ICNN problem learning long-term dependencies full text Jacobian spectral radius proof content"`
