## Item 1 — Elman 1988 vs. 1990: are they architecturally different?

**Verdict: CONFIRMED AS SAME ARCHITECTURE. The 1988 CRL Technical Report 8903 and the 1990 *Cognitive Science* paper present the same SRN. No architectural difference identified. Context-to-hidden weights are fixed (copy, weight=1.0) in both versions.**

The UCSD Center for Research in Language (CRL) newsletter (crl.ucsd.edu/newsletter/5-2/Article1.html) explicitly cites both versions in its references:

> [ELMAN89] Elman, J.L. (1989). *Representation and structure in connectionist models.* CRL Technical Report 8903, Center for Research in Language, University of California, San Diego.
> [ELMAN90] Elman, J.L. (1990). Finding structure in time. *Cognitive Science*, 14, 179–211.

The CRL newsletter commentary discusses both papers as describing the same model and architecture across two experimental variations (lexical category simulation and grammatical structure simulation). There is no reference to any architectural revision between the 1989 report and the 1990 journal paper. The 1990 paper is the journal publication of work first circulated as the 1989 CRL technical report — not the 1988 Semantic Scholar date sometimes cited.

**Note on the 1988 date:** The Semantic Scholar record (semanticscholar.org, CorpusID:2763403) shows a publication date of “1 March 1990” for the *Cognitive Science* paper, not 1988. Any reference to an Elman 1988 SRN source may be a misattribution. The earliest confirmed preprint is CRL Technical Report 8903 (1989). The chapter should not cite an "Elman 1988" source without locating that specific document.

**Confirmed citations for Ch.06:**
> Jeffrey L. Elman, "Representation and structure in connectionist models," CRL Technical Report 8903, Center for Research in Language, UC San Diego, 1989. [Preprint / working paper]  
> Jeffrey L. Elman, "Finding Structure in Time," *Cognitive Science*, 14(2), pp. 179–211, March 1990. [Definitive journal version]

**⚠️ OPEN:** The Semantic Scholar record for the 1990 paper cites Jordan (1986) as the prior proposal from which Elman extended: "A proposal along these lines first described by Jordan (1986) which involves the use of recurrent links in order to provide networks with a dynamic memory." This cross-confirms the Jordan → Elman lineage but does not resolve whether there is a separate 1988 document. If ARC editorial team has access to Semantic Scholar’s full record, the 1988 date should be traced to a specific document ID.

## Item 2 — Jordan 1986 ICS 8604 vs. Jordan 1997 *AI*: architectural differences?

**Verdict: ⚠️ PARTIALLY RESOLVED. The 1986 ICS 8604 report is confirmed and well-characterised (see ARC-20). No 1997 *Artificial Intelligence* paper by Jordan on serial order has been confirmed in public sources. The 1997 citation may be a misattribution or confusion with a different Jordan paper.**

The 1986 ICS 8604 report (*Serial Order: A Parallel Distributed Processing Approach*) is confirmed from multiple PDFs (UCSD CSE, baulab.info, CMU Simon Archive, Otterbein University mirror at faculty.otterbein.edu). The report’s Figure 3 and Table 1 define the architecture: plan units + state units as input; recurrent connections from output to state units; connections between state units. Key note from the 1986 report itself (cseweb.ucsd.edu/~gary/PAPER-SUGGESTIONS/Jordan-TR-8604-OCRed.pdf):

> "The recurrent connections from the output units to the state units are not necessary for the operation of the network, given that there are connections between the state units."

This is architecturally significant: Jordan’s own 1986 report acknowledges the state-unit connections as the primary memory mechanism, with output-to-state connections as optional. This nuance is absent from most secondary sources.

A Jordan paper in *Artificial Intelligence* (1997) does not appear in any source searched. Jordan’s major 1997 contributions were in the *Machine Learning* journal (HMM / mixture-of-experts work) and his 1997 MIT Press book chapter ("Serial Order"). The 1997 *Artificial Intelligence* citation may conflate Jordan’s 1997 book chapter with a journal article, or may be an error in the ARC-6 research note.

**⚠️ OPEN:** The specific Jordan 1997 *Artificial Intelligence* citation cannot be confirmed from available public sources. Suggested follow-up query: `"Michael Jordan 1997 Artificial Intelligence journal recurrent neural network serial order"`. If this citation cannot be verified, the draft should rely exclusively on the 1986 ICS 8604 report.

## Item 3 — Werbos 1974 Harvard PhD thesis: BPTT content confirmed

**Verdict: CONFIRMED. Werbos (1974) contains the first derivation of BPTT for recurrent networks. Content is in Chapter 5 of the thesis. The CMU-hosted Werbos (1990) paper at cmu.edu/~bhiksha/deeplearning describes the algorithm and explicitly traces its origin to the 1974 thesis.**

This item substantially overlaps with findings from ARC-20 (Item 4) and ARC-25 (Item 1). Key additions from the CMU-hosted Werbos (1990) PDF (cs.cmu.edu/~bhiksha/courses/deeplearning/Fall.2016/pdfs/Werbos.backprop.pdf):

> "Backpropagation can be applied to any system with a well-defined order of calculations, even if those calculations depend on past calculations within the network itself."

The paper then gives a worked example of BPTT with a recurrent network (Figure 5) where "every neuron is potentially allowed to input values from any of the neurons at the two previous time periods." The 1990 paper is explicitly a journal formalisation of work first described in the 1974 thesis.

The BPTT chapter in the dlsi.ua.es online textbook (dlsi.ua.es/~mlf/nnafmc/pbook/node28.html) confirms the textbook consensus:
> "The most commonly used reference for BPTT is the book chapter by Rumelhart et al. (1986), although an earlier description of BPTT may be found in **Werbos (1974)**’s PhD dissertation (see also Werbos (1990))."

**Definitive confirmed timeline (combining ARC-20, ARC-25, ARC-26):**

| Year | Author | Work | BPTT content |
|------|--------|------|--------------|
| 1974 | Werbos | *Beyond Regression*, Harvard PhD | First derivation; Chapter 5, section 5.5.1 |
| 1985/86 | Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams | UCSD ICS / *Nature* | Re-derivation; definitive popularisation |
| 1990 | Werbos | *Proceedings of the IEEE*, 78(10) | Formal journal exposition of 1974 work |

**Confirmed citations for Ch.06:**
> Paul J. Werbos, *Beyond Regression: New Tools for Prediction and Analysis in the Behavioral Sciences*, Ph.D. dissertation, Harvard University, 1974.  
> Paul J. Werbos, "Backpropagation through time: what it does and how to do it," *Proceedings of the IEEE*, 78(10), pp. 1550–1560, October 1990.

## Item 4 — Bengio et al. (1994): exact sequence-length threshold for gradient vanishing

**Verdict: ⚠️ THRESHOLD IS THEORETICAL/PROOF-BASED, NOT A SINGLE EMPIRICAL NUMBER. Bengio et al. prove that gradient vanishing is exponential in sequence length for robust long-term storage. The “5–10 steps” claim in secondary sources is an approximation, not a verbatim finding from the paper.**

The confirmed publication metadata (oamonitor.ireland.openaire.eu and dlsi.ua.es):
> Y. Bengio, P. Simard, P. Frasconi, "Learning Long-Term Dependencies with Gradient Descent is Difficult," *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks*, 5(2), pp. 157–166, March 1994.

The dlsi.ua.es BPTT companion paper commentary (dlsi.ua.es/~mlf/nnafmc/pbook/node41.html) accurately characterises the paper’s structure:

> "Bengio et al. (1994) **prove** that the vanishing of gradients is especially severe when we want the DTRNN to robustly store information about a very early effect. The paper also presents **a series of experiments** in which the performance of alternate DTRNN learning methods is evaluated for **three simple single-input single-output problems** having long-term dependencies; the experiments show a partial success of some of them."

The paper’s contribution is therefore dual:
1. **Theoretical proof:** Gradient vanishing is proven to be exponential in the length of the dependency — a formal result, not an empirical threshold.
2. **Experiments:** Three benchmark tasks. The paper shows failure to learn long-term dependencies in standard BPTT/RTRL; alternative methods partially succeed.

The canonical abstract (oamonitor source) confirms: "We show why gradient based learning algorithms face an increasingly difficult problem as the **duration of the dependencies to be captured increases**. These results expose a **trade-off** between efficient learning by gradient descent and latching on information for long periods."

**The "~5–10 steps" claim:** This approximation circulates in lecture notes and textbooks (e.g., the 2016 CMSC 35246 notes already cited in ARC-20). It is not a verbatim result from Bengio et al. (1994). The paper characterises the problem as one that worsens with dependency length without giving a universal threshold — because the threshold depends on the spectral radius of the weight matrix, not a fixed sequence length.

**Position statement for Ch.06 draft author:**  
Do not assert "RNNs lose gradient signal after 5–10 steps" as a Bengio et al. finding. The correct formulation is: Bengio et al. (1994) prove that gradient-based learning of dependencies over sequences of length T faces a problem that grows exponentially with T — making long-range dependencies (T >> 1) practically unlearnable by standard BPTT. For the chapter’s narrative, "10 to 20 steps" is a commonly cited practical upper bound, but this should be attributed to later empirical work, not to the 1994 paper.

**⚠️ OPEN:** A specific empirical sequence-length figure from Bengio et al.’s three benchmark tasks requires access to the full paper (behind IEEE paywall). Suggested query: `"Bengio 1994 Table 1 Table 2 results sequence length RNN performance latching"`. Physical or institutional access to *IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks* 5(2) required.
