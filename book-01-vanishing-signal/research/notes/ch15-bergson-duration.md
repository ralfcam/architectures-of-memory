## Findings

### 1. *Time and Free Will* (1889): *durée réelle* and the critique of spatialised time

Bergson's first major work, the doctoral thesis published as *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience* (1889) — translated by F. L. Pogson as *Time and Free Will: An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness* (George Allen & Unwin, 1910) — introduces the distinction that governs the rest of his philosophy: the difference between *durée réelle* (real duration) and the homogeneous, spatialisable time of natural science and common sense. The Philosophy Now summary (philosophynow.org, issue 48) captures the core move precisely: Bergson argued that the mechanistic time of science "is based on a misperception: it consists of superimposing spatial concepts onto time, which then becomes a distorted version of the real thing." Scientific time, he held, treats moments as discrete points on a line — measurable, reversible, interchangeable. But lived time, *durée*, is qualitatively heterogeneous: each moment interpenetrates the next, and no state of consciousness can ever recur exactly because the very passage of time has changed the subject who experiences it.

The Studylib summary of Pogson's translator's introduction articulates the technical definition: duration is "a succession without distinction, an interpenetration of elements so heterogeneous that former states can never recur." The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Bergson (plato.stanford.edu) situates this as Bergson's core epistemological programme: to "un-mix" time and space, to differentiate the immediate data of consciousness (which are temporal) from the spatial representations that philosophy and science impose on them. The SEP notes that in duration there is "no juxtaposition of events; therefore there is no mechanistic causality" — which is Bergson's route to a non-deterministic account of freedom. Free will is possible because the future is not already laid out as a spatial map; it emerges from a duration that is genuinely open.

Bergson's own preface (Brock University, brocku.ca) gives the thesis in a single sentence: "all discussion between the determinists and their opponents implies a previous confusion of duration with extensity, of succession with simultaneity, of quality with quantity: this confusion once dispelled, we may perhaps witness the disappearance of the objections raised against free will." The philosophical import of this for the book's ML mirror thread is considerable: Bergson's argument is that the mistake is *conceptual* before it is empirical. You do not resolve the problem of free will by running better experiments; you resolve it by recognising that the experimental method, in presupposing measurable, homogeneous time, has already assumed away the very phenomenon it purports to study. The parallel in ML: you do not resolve catastrophic forgetting by scaling up memory; you resolve it by recognising that the model's relationship to its own training history is not well described by the homogeneous time of the training loop counter.

For the ML mirror thread: *durée réelle* maps onto the model's *implicit temporal structure* — the way in which earlier training shapes the loss landscape for later training in ways that are not captured by any explicit timestamp or step counter. The training loop treats time as homogeneous (step N is interchangeable with step N+1 given the same batch); *durée* insists that the model at step N+1 is not the same learner as at step N, because step N has already changed the landscape. Continual learning systems that treat all training steps as equivalent are committing Bergson's error: spatialising time.

### 2. *Matter and Memory* (1896): habit-memory vs. pure memory and the architecture of the past

Bergson's second major work, *Matière et mémoire* (1896), translated by Nancy Margaret Paul and W. Scott Palmer (George Allen & Unwin, 1911), develops the most sophisticated and directly relevant theory of memory in the pre-cognitive-science philosophical canon. The Wikipedia entry on *Matter and Memory* (en.wikipedia.org) identifies the two types of memory at the heart of the book:

- **Habit-memory** (*mémoire-habitude*): the condensed, motor residue of repeated past actions. Bergson takes as his example learning a poem by repetition: what is retained is not a dated episode but a motor-dispositional schema, enacted without conscious effort. This is the memory of the body; it is "habit interpreted by memory" or "habit more than memory," as Bergson puts it (PhilPapers, philpapers.org/archive/PERBPO.pdf).
- **Pure memory** (*souvenir pur*): the spontaneous, non-motor representation of a specific past event, recognised as past. Bergson's example is the recollection of *first* learning the poem — the memory of the occasion, not the motor schema. Pure memory is "of a contemplative and fundamentally spiritual kind" (Wikipedia, *Matter and Memory*); it is the representation of the event in its unique, dated singularity. It cannot be repeated; it can only be remembered.

The Protevi lecture notes on *Matter and Memory* Chapter 3 (protevi.com) add the crucial structural claim: pure memory and habit-memory are not two separate faculties but two extreme poles of a single memory-duration. All memory lies on a spectrum between pure contemplative recollection (the virtual past) and pure bodily habit (the actual present). The unifying term is duration itself: "it is duration itself that assures the unity of the two memories" (Frédéric Worms, *Bergson ou les deux sens de la vie*, quoted in PhilPapers).

For the organisational mirror thread: Bergson's two-memory distinction is the most precise philosophical tool in the series for diagnosing organisational knowledge loss. **Habit-memory** ≈ organisational routines, standard operating procedures, workflow automatisms — knowledge encoded in process rather than in explicit representation. This knowledge is durable, transferable, documentable. **Pure memory** ≈ the situated, episodic knowledge held by individuals: *why* this client relationship is structured the way it is, *what happened* in the 2018 restructuring and who was involved, *how* the original system architecture decision was made and what alternatives were rejected. This knowledge is virtual, non-repeatable, and dies with the individual who holds it. The organisational pathos of Chs.10–11 is the pathos of losing pure memory while retaining the illusion that habit-memory (process documentation) is sufficient. It is not sufficient.

For the ML mirror thread: habit-memory ≈ the model's frozen weight matrix — the residue of all past gradient updates, compressed into a static dispositional structure that produces outputs without recalling specific training examples. Pure memory ≈ the training data itself, or more precisely, the episodic record of which examples produced which gradient updates in which sequence. No production ML system retains pure memory; all production ML systems operate on habit-memory alone. Catastrophic forgetting is the failure mode of a system that has *only* habit-memory: when new habit-memory is written, the old is overwritten. A system with genuine pure memory (e.g., an episodic replay buffer, or a retrieval-augmented architecture with a persistent document store) would not catastrophically forget, because it could re-actualise the past episode on demand. The chapter should use Bergson's framework to argue that the search for a catastrophic-forgetting solution is the search for a *computational analogue of pure memory*.

### 3. The Bergson–Einstein debate (1922): lived duration vs. physical time and the limits of the philosopher's intervention

On April 22, 1922 — exactly 104 years before this research note — the *Société française de Philosophie* hosted Albert Einstein in Paris. Bergson, who was at the time completing *Durée et Simultanéité: à propos de la théorie d'Einstein* (1922), intervened in the discussion. The JFFP article (jffp.pitt.edu) provides the best primary-source context: "In the course of this discussion, Henri Bergson, who was at that time writing *Duration and Simultaneity*, which explored some of the philosophical implications of Einstein's theory, was asked to share his thoughts. The resulting remarks offer a glimpse into Bergson's analysis of the concept of simultaneity, and Einstein's brief reply reveals his insistence that time itself, not just 'the physicist's time,' is relative."

The HAL/web.archive.org essay by Cochet (hal-02423694) gives the fullest account of Bergson's argument in *Durée et Simultanéité*: Bergson did not deny special relativity's mathematical results; he argued that the *philosopher's time* (lived duration, the time of consciousness) was in harmony with the universal simultaneity that Newton had posited, and that Einstein's relativisation of simultaneity was a *physical* result that applied to the physicist's constructed time — not to duration as lived. The De Gruyter essay "The Test of Time" (degruyter.com, 10.1515/9783110753707-011) makes the philosophical stakes clear: "From Bergson's point of view, there is no way to grasp the real nature of time but by living it; we can only start from the intuition we have of our own conscious, lived [duration]." Einstein's reply, as reported in the JFFP, was blunt: there is only one time, and it is relative.

The outcome of the debate is well documented: Einstein won decisively among physicists, and Bergson's reputation suffered. Bergson reportedly requested that he not be cited in the Nobel Prize citation for Einstein in 1921 (the prize was for the photoelectric effect, not relativity), and *Durée et Simultanéité* was the one work Bergson asked not to be reprinted during his lifetime. The De Gruyter neuroscience essay (10.1515/9783110753707-019) represents the contemporary rehabilitation: Bergson's dynamic view of consciousness as time-constituting "complements embodied models of time consciousness" in contemporary cognitive neuroscience, where the experience of duration is governed by "emotional and visceral processes which share a common underlying neural processing." The rehabilitation does not salvage Bergson's physics, but it does salvage his phenomenology.

For the ML mirror thread: the Bergson–Einstein debate maps onto the tension between *wall-clock time* (the training loop counter, the timestamp of a data point) and *functional time* (the model's internal representation of temporal structure, which may be highly non-linear). A model trained on a corpus ordered by publication date is exposed to a monotonically increasing wall-clock time; but the *functional time* of the model — how much the model's representations change per unit of training — is highly non-uniform. Early training produces large representation changes; later training produces small ones. The model's internal duration is not proportional to the step counter. Bergson would say: the model has a *durée*, and it cannot be read off from the clock.

For the organisational mirror thread: the debate maps onto the gap between *calendar time* (organisational age, years in operation, milestone dates) and *functional time* (the actual pace of organisational learning, knowledge accumulation, and institutional memory formation). An organisation that has been in operation for 50 years may have the functional memory of a 5-year-old if it has undergone repeated restructuring. Calendar time and functional duration diverge, exactly as Einstein's relativistic time and Bergson's lived duration diverge.

### 4. Deleuze's *Bergsonism* (1966): the virtual, multiplicity, and the rehabilitation of duration for contemporary theory

Gilles Deleuze's *Le Bergsonisme* (1966), translated by Hugh Tomlinson and Barbara Habberjam (Zone Books, 1988), is the authoritative modern interpretation of Bergson and the primary vehicle through which Bergson entered late 20th-century Continental and posthumanist theory. The Academia.edu essay on Deleuze's Bergsonism (academia.edu/30644074) establishes Deleuze's central move: he reconstructs Bergson's concepts of *intuition*, *virtuality*, and *duration* to critique "traditional science and metaphysics' insular obsession with quantitative differences, succession, homogeneity." Deleuze's reading identifies what he calls the *method of intuition* as Bergson's most important contribution: the procedure of grasping duration directly, from the inside, rather than reconstructing it from external, spatialised snapshots.

The key Deleuzian innovation is the concept of the **virtual**: for Deleuze, Bergson's pure memory (the integral conservation of the past in an unconscious, virtual state) is not a psychological phenomenon but an *ontological* one. The past is not stored in a brain; it is preserved in a virtual dimension of Being. The virtual is real but not actual; it is the reservoir from which the present actualises itself. The Pli journal essay by Ansell-Pearson (plijournal.com) confirms Deleuze's insistence on this point in the 1966 text: "throughout the 1966 text Deleuze will insist on the need to posit virtual multiplicity as a single time, and the co-existence of all of the degrees and levels of Being is said to be virtual and 'only virtual'." Keith Ansell-Pearson (University of Warwick) is the leading Anglophone scholar of Bergson-via-Deleuze; his *Philosophy and the Adventure of the Virtual: Bergson and the Time of Life* (Routledge, 2002) is the standard secondary text.

Deleuze replaces Bergson's possible/real opposition with a virtual/actual distinction: the possible is a degraded real (a retroactive projection of what was already real onto an imagined antecedent state), while the virtual is genuinely creative — it is not the pre-given form of what will be actualised, but the open multiplicity from which actualisation proceeds by *differentiation*. This is Deleuze's solution to the problem of novelty: genuine novelty requires virtuality, not mere possibility.

For the ML mirror thread: the virtual/actual distinction maps with precision onto the distinction between a model's **latent space** (virtual: a high-dimensional representation of all possible inputs and outputs, never directly observed, compressed into weight space) and its **forward pass outputs** (actual: specific predictions, token sequences, embeddings produced by a given input). The latent space is virtual in Deleuze's sense — it is real (it determines all outputs) but never directly accessed; it is the reservoir of the model's compressed world-model. Continual learning, on this reading, is the problem of *updating the virtual* without destroying its coherence: the challenge is not to overwrite the latent space with new data but to *differentiate* it, to grow new dimensions of virtual multiplicity without collapsing existing ones. This gives a precise philosophical formulation of the problem that parameter isolation methods (PackNet, progressive neural networks) are trying to solve.

For the organisational mirror thread: the virtual/actual distinction maps onto the difference between an organisation's **tacit knowledge base** (virtual: the sum of all its members' know-how, judgements, contextual awareness — never fully articulated, never directly observable) and its **explicit outputs** (actual: reports, decisions, products, communications). Knowledge management's persistent failure is the attempt to actualise the virtual wholesale — to convert all tacit knowledge into explicit documentation. Deleuze (via Bergson) would say this is incoherent: the virtual cannot be fully actualised without being destroyed; it must be *used* to differentiate, not *extracted* to be preserved.

## Narrative threads for Ch.15

1. **The two times** — open with the contrast between the training loop counter (homogeneous, spatial, reversible) and the model's actual representational history (heterogeneous, cumulative, irreversible). Bergson names this gap in 1889; continual learning rediscovers it in 2017. The chapter's irony: the engineers built a system that runs on Einstein's time but learns on Bergson's.
2. **Habit-memory as the weight matrix** — use Bergson's two-memory distinction to frame the chapter's core claim: all current ML systems operate on habit-memory alone. The weight matrix is the perfect Bergsonian habit: it is the condensed residue of all past training, wholly absorbed into a present dispositional structure, with no trace of the individual episodes that produced it. Catastrophic forgetting is the pathology of a system that has only habit-memory.
3. **Pure memory as the design requirement** — the chapter's constructive move: what would a system with genuine pure memory look like? Episodic replay buffers, retrieval-augmented generation, persistent document stores. Each is an attempt to build a computational analogue of Bergson's *souvenir pur*: a record of the past that retains the past's dated, situated, non-repeatable character.
4. **The 1922 debate as a parable for the book** — Bergson argues that science's time is not the only real time; Einstein replies that there is only one time. The book's argument is that *for learning systems*, Bergson was right and Einstein was wrong: the learning system's functional duration is not reducible to the clock. The parable also carries a warning: Bergson lost the argument in 1922 because he could not specify what his lived duration *does* that Einstein's time cannot account for. The chapter must not make the same mistake; it must be specific about what *durée* implies for the design of continual learning systems.
5. **Deleuze's virtual as the latent space** — close the chapter with the virtual/actual distinction as a design principle: the goal of continual learning is not to preserve all past outputs (actual) but to maintain the coherence of the virtual latent space while allowing it to differentiate. This gives a precise philosophical target for evaluation: a continual learning system succeeds if it preserves the topology of the latent space for previously learned tasks while allowing new dimensions to open.
