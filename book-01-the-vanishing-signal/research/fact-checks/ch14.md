## Findings

### 1. Hobbes *De Corpore* — Chapter 11 locus, section number, Molesworth edition, and the Argo question

**Locus confirmed:** The Ship of Theseus passage is in *De Corpore* (1655), **Part II, Chapter 11, §7** — "Of Identity and Difference." This is verified by the University of Colorado Philosophy Department PDF (carneades.pomona.edu), which reproduces the passage under the heading: "From Thomas Hobbes, *Elements of Philosophy* (1655) Part II, Ch. 11, §7." The rintintin.colorado.edu PDF gives the header: "ON SAMENESS OF IDENTITY. By Thomas Hobbes. From *De Corpore* (1655) part 2, chapter 11," confirming the same locus. The full text of §7 begins: "But the same body may at different times be compared with itself. And from hence springs a great controversy among philosophers about the beginning of individuation..."

**Molesworth edition:** The standard critical edition is **William Molesworth, ed., *The English Works of Thomas Hobbes of Malmesbury*, 11 vols. (London: John Bohn, 1839–1845)**. *De Corpore* (English translation, *Elements of Philosophy, the First Section, Concerning Body*) appears in **Volume I**. The Online Library of Liberty and Internet Archive (onlinebooks.library.upenn.edu, archive.org) both confirm this 11-volume edition as the canonical reference. The chapter/section citation is therefore: Hobbes, *De Corpore*, Part II, Ch. 11, §7, in *English Works*, vol. I, ed. Molesworth (London, 1839).

**Page number in Molesworth Vol. I:** The precise page number for §7 of Ch. 11 in Molesworth Vol. I has not been confirmed from the primary text in this research cycle. The homepages.uc.edu PDF of the Molesworth *De Corpore* is the closest available source but is paginated from the reprint, not the original Molesworth numbering.

> ⚠️ Flagged: Molesworth Vol. I page number for *De Corpore* Part II, Ch. 11, §7 requires verification from the physical Molesworth edition or a scan with original pagination. Suggested query: `"Molesworth English Works Hobbes Volume I De Corpore Part II Chapter 11 section 7 page number"`

**The Argo question — CRITICAL CORRECTION:** The ARC-34 issue body states that "Wikipedia notes Hobbes refers to the ship as the *Argo* rather than Theseus's galley." This requires careful qualification. Multiple primary source reproductions (rintintin.colorado.edu, carneades.pomona.edu) confirm that Hobbes in *De Corpore* Part II, Ch. 11, §7 explicitly uses "**that Ship of Theseus**" — not the Argo. The Argo reference appears in a *different* text: Hobbes's *De Mundo Examined* (examination of Thomas White's *De Mundo*, written c. 1642–3, not published until 1973), Chapter 12, §§3–4, which is reproduced in the rintintin.colorado.edu PDF immediately after the *De Corpore* excerpt under a clearly separate header. The two passages are distinct. The philarchive.org paper by Adriano confirms this: Hobbes used the Ship of Theseus in *De Corpore* and a different ship example in other contexts.

**Verdict for Ch.14:** The chapter should cite *De Corpore*, Part II, Ch. 11, §7, Molesworth ed. Vol. I (1839). The ship is named as "the Ship of Theseus" by Hobbes himself in this passage — no Argo confusion exists in *De Corpore* specifically. If the chapter also uses the *De Mundo* passage, it must cite it separately (ch. 12, §§3–4, trans. Harold Whitmore Jones, London, 1976). The Wikipedia Argo note likely conflates the two Hobbes texts.

### 3. Wiggins 1980 vs. 2001 — edition differences, chapter-by-chapter changes

**Bibliographic details confirmed:**
- **1980 edition:** David Wiggins, *Sameness and Substance* (Oxford: Blackwell / Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1980). [Note: The 1980 edition was published by Blackwell in the UK and Harvard UP in the US.]
- **2001 edition:** David Wiggins, *Sameness and Substance Renewed* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2001). ISBN 0-521-45619-3 (paperback), 9780521456197. 257 pp. Confirmed by Google Books (web:844): "Publisher, Cambridge University Press, 2001; ISBN, 0521456193."

**Substantive differences — confirmed from Weatherson review (MIT PDF, web:830) and Weatherson's blog (web:842):**

The 2001 edition is, in effect, a revised second edition of the 1980 book. Key differences:

1. **Chapters 1–6 (bulk of 2001):** Most changes are confined to footnotes — additions citing and briefly commenting on post-1980 publications. Substantive content of the 1980 arguments is largely preserved.
2. **Penultimate chapter (new in 2001):** A "mostly new discussion of the determinacy of identity" — not present in 1980.
3. **Final chapter (personal identity) — MAJOR REVISION:** "Wiggins retracts many of the claims made in the matching chapter of 1980, and raises some interesting objections to Parfit's account of personal identity." This is the most significant substantive change. If Ch.14 cites Wiggins on personal identity, it must cite the **2001 edition** for the revised position, and distinguish it from the 1980 position if both are used.
4. **Stylistic changes:** Otherwise mainly stylistic; the preface quips it is "a matter of no concern whether 1980 and 2001 are the same book" (Wiggins's own joke, noted by Weatherson).
5. **2012 Wiggins paper** (Wiley Online, web:826): Wiggins's 2012 paper "Identity, Individuation and Substance" explicitly corrects and extends "certain theses or formulations proposed in the author's *Sameness and Substance Renewed* (2001)" — confirming the 2001 edition itself is now partially superseded for specific technical claims.

**Verdict for Ch.14:** The chapter must cite the **2001 edition** for all claims about personal identity (the 1980 personal identity chapter is retracted). For claims about sortal-dependence of identity and persistence conditions (the core Wiggins thesis), either edition's argument is acceptable, but the footnote base of 2001 is richer. Specify clearly: cite as Wiggins (2001) for the revised positions; cite as Wiggins (1980) only if explicitly discussing the pre-2001 position that was later retracted.

## Narrative threads for Ch.14

1. **Hobbes invents the puzzle, Sider solves it with time-slices, Kirkpatrick solves it with weight constraints:** The chapter has a clean 360-year argumentative arc from *De Corpore* §7 to EWC. The persistence problem that Hobbes posed for physical bodies reappears as the stability-plasticity tradeoff in neural networks — the same formal structure across three centuries.
2. **Wiggins's retraction as institutional memory failure:** The fact that Wiggins publicly retracted his 1980 personal identity chapter in 2001 (and has since extended corrections in 2012) enacts the book's thesis about revisable institutional memory. His preface joke — "it is a matter of no concern whether 1980 and 2001 are the same book" — is a gift to the chapter.
3. **Sider's two editions and the pagination trap:** The existence of a 2001 hardback (280 pp.) and a 2003 paperback (255 pp.) with different paginations is a practical demonstration of the identity problem the book is discussing. The chapter can use this self-referentially.
4. **EWC as Hobbes vindicated:** Hobbes argued that the identity of a thing with intermittent existence depends on its form, not its matter. EWC preserves the "form" (task-relevant weight configurations) while replacing the "matter" (other weights). Kirkpatrick et al. operationalise the Hobbesian position without citing it.
5. **The two Hobbes ship texts:** That Hobbes used the Ship of Theseus in *De Corpore* (1655, published) and a different ship in *De Mundo* (c. 1642, unpublished until 1973) suggests the puzzle was a sustained preoccupation across his career — not a single intervention.
