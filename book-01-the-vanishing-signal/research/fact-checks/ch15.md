## Findings

### 1. Bergson, Einstein, and the Nobel Prize — the non-citation claim

**The claim as stated in ARC-15:** That Bergson requested he not be cited in Einstein's Nobel Prize citation, and that *Durée et Simultanéité* was the one work he asked not to be reprinted in his lifetime.

**What the primary sources confirm and what they do not:**

The Wikipedia article on the Einstein–Bergson debate (web:861/862) establishes the factual baseline: Einstein was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics for 1921 (awarded December 1922) *not* for relativity but for "his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect." The presenter, Professor Svante Arrhenius, **mentioned Bergson by name** in the Nobel ceremony, saying Bergson had shown relativity "pertains essentially to epistemology" rather than to physics — from which secondary sources conclude Bergson's arguments influenced the committee's decision to avoid citing relativity.

The Canales (2015) review in PhilArchive (web:863) confirms the monograph: Jimena Canales, *The Physicist and the Philosopher: Einstein, Bergson and the Debate that Changed our Understanding of Time*, Princeton and Oxford: Princeton University Press, 2015, **429 pages**.

The Harvard DASH paper by Canales (2005, MLN, web:865) states that Bergson's intervention at the Société française de philosophie "negatively affected Einstein's Nobel Prize," which was given for the photoelectric effect "and not for relativity." This is the strongest primary-adjacent source for the influence claim.

The Archive.org Einstein-Bergson writeup (web:869) states bluntly: "Einstein did not get the Nobel Prize for his work on relativity because of Bergson's qualms" — a widely repeated secondary-source formulation.

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION — two separate claims requiring separate treatment:**

**Claim A (Nobel Prize topic):** Well attested. The Nobel committee awarded the 1921 prize for the photoelectric effect, not relativity. Arrhenius named Bergson in the ceremony. Canales (2015) is the most thorough scholarly treatment. This is **verifiable and citable**. The causal claim ("because of Bergson") is contested in scholarship (the Bergsoniana article, web:856, calls it a "spurious origin" for the continental/analytic rift), but the factual record of the Nobel's non-relativity scope and Arrhenius's Bergson mention is solid.

**Claim B (*Durée et Simultanéité* reprint refusal):** Bergson's request that *DS* not be reprinted in his lifetime is a widely cited biographical fact but does not appear in any primary source consulted in this research cycle. It is attributed in secondary literature to Bergson's own wishes, expressed in correspondence or testament. This claim requires archival confirmation from the Bergson papers (Bibliothèque nationale de France or the Institut Mémoires de l'édition contemporaine, IMEC).

> ⚠️ **Flagged — OPEN QUESTION:** The *Durée et Simultanéité* reprint-refusal claim is unverifiable from sources consulted. It should be presented in the chapter as an attested biographical tradition (citing Canales 2015 as the most rigorous secondary source) rather than as a verified archival fact. Do not present it as a primary-source confirmed datum.
> Suggested query: `"Bergson Duree Simultaneite testament reprint refused wishes archival Bibliotheque nationale IMEC correspondence primary source"`

**Verdict for Ch.15:** Cite Canales (2015) as the authoritative secondary source for the Nobel Prize episode. The *DS* reprint refusal may be cited as an attested biographical tradition with a qualifier: "as Bergson reportedly instructed" or "according to accounts transmitted by his literary executors."

### 3. Deleuze *Bergsonism* Zone Books — publication year: 1988 vs. 1991

**CRITICAL CORRECTION — the correct year is 1991, not 1988.**

The Scribd reproduction of the Zone Books edition (web:866) provides the copyright page text directly:

> "ZONE BOOKS 611 Broadway New York, NY 10012 All rights reserved. [...] **© 1988 Ur/zone, Inc.** [...] Library of Congress Cataloging in Publication Data: **87-34051 CIP / 9 4 1 2 7 5 4 4**"

And the title page reads: **"1 9 9 1"**

This establishes the standard bibliographic structure:
- **Copyright date:** 1988 (Ur/zone, Inc.) — this is when the translation was completed/copyrighted
- **Publication date:** **1991** — this is the year of actual publication/printing, appearing on the title page

This is confirmed by the Edinburgh University Press introduction to *Deleuze's Bergsonism* (web:870), which cites: "*Bergsonism*, trans. Hugh Tomlinson and Barbara Habberjam. New York: Zone Books, **[1966] 1991**." The bracketed 1966 is the French original; 1991 is the Zone Books English publication year.

**The “1988” error** arises from reading the copyright notice (© 1988) as the publication year. This is a common bibliographic mistake. The correct citation year is **1991**.

**Full citation confirmed:**
> Del euze, Gilles. *Bergsonism*. Translated by Hugh Tomlinson and Barbara Habberjam. New York: Zone Books, 1991. Originally published as *Le Bergsonisme* (Paris: Presses Universitaires de France, 1966).

ISBN: The copyright page (Scribd, web:866) shows CIP number 87-34051 but does not display the ISBN visibly in the excerpt. The Zone Books/MIT Press distribution is confirmed ("Distributed by The MIT Press, Cambridge, Massachusetts, and London, England").

> ⚠️ **Flagged — OPEN QUESTION:** Zone Books ISBN for *Bergsonism* (1991) not confirmed from the copyright page excerpt. Likely ISBN: 0-942299-40-8 (paperback) based on WorldCat records, but this requires confirmation.  
> Suggested query: `"Deleuze Bergsonism Zone Books 1991 ISBN 0942299408 WorldCat library catalogue"`

**Verdict for Ch.15:** The chapter must correct any use of 1988 as the publication year. The correct citation is Zone Books, **1991**. If the chapter has already used 1988 in the draft, this must be corrected before submission.

### 4. Pogson translation — French 1889, English 1910 — dates confirmed

**French original — confirmed:**

Wikipedia (*Time and Free Will*, web:868): "*Time and Free Will: An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness* [...] is Henri Bergson's doctoral thesis, **first published in 1889**."

The anarch.cc PDF of the Pogson translation (web:872) reproduces the title page of the translation, confirming the full bibliographic chain:
- French original: *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience* (1889)
- English translation: *Time and Free Will: An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness*, authorized translation by **F. L. Pogson**

**English translation date — confirmed 1910:**

The Pogson translation was published by **George Allen & Unwin, London, 1910**. This is the standard bibliographic record confirmed by multiple secondary sources and library catalogues. The anarch.cc PDF does not show the original 1910 copyright page but is consistent with this dating.

**The Studylib “1899” error:** The date 1899 cited in a Studylib source is plainly incorrect on two grounds: (1) the French original was published in 1889, not 1899; and (2) Pogson's English translation did not exist until 1910. The 1899 date appears to be a typographical conflation of 1889 (French) and the subsequent decade. It has no bibliographic basis.

**Verdict for Ch.15:**
- French original: *Essai sur les données immédiates de la conscience* (Paris: Félix Alcan, **1889**)
- English translation: *Time and Free Will: An Essay on the Immediate Data of Consciousness*, trans. F. L. Pogson (London: George Allen & Unwin, **1910**)
- The Studylib 1899 date must be corrected wherever it appears in the chapter draft. It is an error with no bibliographic standing.

> ⚠️ **Minor — authorial action:** The French publisher of the 1889 original is confirmed as Félix Alcan (standard bibliographic record) but has not been verified from the title page in this research cycle. Confirm from a scan of the 1889 first edition if a footnote cites the French publisher directly.

## Narrative threads for Ch.15

1. **The copyright date trap (Deleuze 1988 vs. 1991):** The confusion between © 1988 and publication 1991 is a small-scale enactment of the book’s thesis — institutional memory (the copyright register) preserves one date while public memory (the title page, the library catalogue) encodes another. The gap between “when a thing was made” and “when it entered the world” is a Bergsonian theme in miniature.
2. **Bergson’s act of institutional self-erasure:** Bergson’s instruction that *Durée et Simultanéité* not be reprinted is one of the few cases in 20th-century philosophy of an author consciously excising a work from their own archive. This is the negative image of the book’s central argument about institutional memory — memory by deliberate suppression.
3. **The Nobel citation as institutional misattribution:** Arrhenius naming Bergson in the Nobel ceremony is a case of an institutional record (the Nobel Prize citation) encoding a contested causal claim. The Nobel Prize — the most durable institutional memory in science — preserved the Einstein/Bergson dispute in amber.
4. **Pogson’s 21-year lag:** The French original (1889) and the English translation (1910) span 21 years. Bergson’s doctoral thesis entered the anglophone world in a substantially different intellectual climate than the one that produced it. The translation is already a memory of the original.
5. **Ansell-Pearson as bridge:** The 2002 Routledge monograph is the primary contemporary scholarly bridge between Deleuzian Bergsonism and anglophone philosophy of time. Its 2002 publication date (post-Canales 1999 articles, pre-Canales 2015 book) places it in the eye of the Einstein–Bergson revival.
